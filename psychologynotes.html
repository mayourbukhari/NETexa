🕉️ Eastern Systems of Psychological Thought

1.1 Bhagavad Gita

📖 Definition

The Bhagavad Gita (Song of the Lord) is a 700-verse Sanskrit text, part of the Indian epic Mahabharata, presenting a dialogue between Prince Arjuna and Lord Krishna. Psychologically, it offers a profound framework for understanding human consciousness, motivation, emotion, and self-regulation.

The Gita addresses fundamental psychological concepts including the nature of self (Atman), the functioning of the mind (Manas), and the path to psychological well-being. It distinguishes between the phenomenal self and the transcendental self, offering insights that parallel modern concepts in transpersonal and humanistic psychology.

Key Psychological Concepts in the Gita
  • Gunas (Three Qualities): Tamas (inertia/ignorance), Rajas (passion/activity), Sattva (purity/harmony) — analogous to personality dimensions
  • Chitta: The mind-stuff or mental substance that records experiences
  • Ahamkara: Ego or I-maker — the source of individual identity
  • Buddhi: Intellect, discriminative wisdom — higher cognitive function
  • Manas: Lower mind dealing with sensory perception and emotional processing
  • Nishkama Karma: Action without attachment to results — relates to intrinsic motivation
  • Yoga of Knowledge (Jnana), Action (Karma), Devotion (Bhakti): Three paths to self-realization
💡 Exam Tip

The Gita's concept of Sthitaprajna (person of steady wisdom) represents an ideal psychological state — stable under pleasure/pain, fear/desire — relevant to modern resilience and emotional regulation concepts. Frequently asked in NET exams.

🌿 Example

The concept of equanimity in the Gita ("samatvam yoga uchyate" — evenness of mind is yoga) parallels modern DBT's emotion regulation and Stoic philosophy's cognitive reappraisal strategies.

1.2 Buddhism

📖 Definition

Buddhist Psychology (Abhidhamma) is a comprehensive analysis of mind and mental factors (cetasikas), developed within Theravada Buddhism. It provides a detailed taxonomy of consciousness and mental states, anticipating many discoveries of modern cognitive science and psychotherapy.

Core Psychological Doctrines
  • Four Noble Truths: Suffering (Dukkha), Origin of suffering (Samudaya), Cessation (Nirodha), Path (Magga) — a clinical problem-solving model
  • Noble Eightfold Path: Right View, Intention, Speech, Action, Livelihood, Effort, Mindfulness, Concentration — a complete behavioral/cognitive intervention
  • Anatman (No-self): The illusion of a permanent self — challenges Western ego psychology
  • Anicca (Impermanence): All phenomena are transient — basis of acceptance-based therapies
  • Dependent Origination (Pratītyasamutpāda): Everything arises in dependence on conditions — systemic/ecological view of behavior
  • Mindfulness (Sati): Non-judgmental present-moment awareness — foundation of MBSR, MBCT, DBT
  • Five Aggregates (Skandhas): Form, Sensation, Perception, Mental formations, Consciousness — Buddhist model of personality
⚠️ Frequently Tested

The Abhidhamma Pitaka catalogues 52 mental factors (cetasikas) and 89/121 types of consciousness. Mindfulness-Based Cognitive Therapy (MBCT) by Segal, Williams & Teasdale draws directly from Buddhist mindfulness practices.

Buddhist Contributions to Modern Psychology
  • Mindfulness-Based Stress Reduction (MBSR) — Jon Kabat-Zinn
  • Acceptance and Commitment Therapy (ACT) — Steven Hayes
  • Dialectical Behavior Therapy (DBT) — Marsha Linehan
  • Positive psychology's concept of "flow" shares parallels with meditative absorption (dhyana)

1.3 Sufism

📖 Definition

Sufism (Tasawwuf) is the mystical dimension of Islam, emphasizing the inner, spiritual aspects of religious life. Psychologically, it focuses on the transformation and purification of the self (nafs) through stages of spiritual development, ultimately seeking union with the Divine.

Key Psychological Concepts in Sufism
  • Nafs (Self/Ego): Seven stages of the self — Ammarah (commanding self, dominated by desires), Lawwamah (self-reproaching), Mulhimah (inspired), Mutmainnah (tranquil), Radiyah (contented), Mardiyyah (pleasing to God), Kamilah (perfect/complete)
  • Qalb (Heart): The spiritual center of consciousness, capable of perceiving divine reality
  • Ruh (Spirit): Divine breath, the transcendent dimension of consciousness
  • Hal (States) & Maqam (Stations): Temporary spiritual experiences vs. permanent attainments — parallels state vs. trait distinction
  • Dhikr (Remembrance): Meditative practice of divine remembrance — therapeutic function similar to mantra meditation
  • Fana (Annihilation) & Baqa (Subsistence): Dissolution of ego and rebirth in higher consciousness — Maslow's peak experiences
💡 Exam Tip

Sufi psychologists like Al-Ghazali (Ihya Ulum al-Din) and Ibn Arabi developed sophisticated theories of consciousness that anticipate modern transpersonal psychology. Al-Ghazali's stages parallel Kohlberg's moral development.

1.4 Integral Yoga (Sri Aurobindo)

📖 Definition

Integral Yoga is a philosophical and spiritual system developed by Sri Aurobindo Ghose (1872–1950) and Mirra Alfassa (The Mother). It posits a multi-layered consciousness and aims at the complete transformation of human nature through the integration of all dimensions of being.

Psychological Framework
  • Levels of Mind: Physical mind, Vital mind, Pure mind, Higher Mind, Illumined Mind, Intuitive Mind, Overmind, Supermind
  • Psychic Being (Chaitya Purusha): The evolving soul, source of psychological growth
  • Subconscient & Inconscient: Analogous to Freud's unconscious but broader in scope
  • Triple Transformation: Psychicization (soul-centered), Spiritualization, Supramentalization
  • Evolution of Consciousness: Matter → Life → Mind → Supermind — a grand developmental psychology
🌿 Example

Sri Aurobindo's concept of the subliminal self is broader than Freud's unconscious, containing not just repressed material but also psychic, spiritual, and cosmic dimensions of being — a "superconscious" above ordinary awareness.

🇮🇳 Academic Psychology in India

1.5 Pre-Independence Era

Psychology as a formal academic discipline in India began in the late 19th and early 20th centuries, primarily under British colonial influence. The establishment of psychology departments mirrored Western institutional models.

1905 — First Psychology Lab

The first experimental psychology laboratory in India was established at the University of Calcutta under N.N. Sengupta, who was trained under Hugo Münsterberg at Harvard.

1915–1930 — Departmental Growth

Psychology departments established at Calcutta, Mysore, Patna universities. Focus on experimental and applied psychology modeled on German/American traditions.

1924 — Sinha's Contribution

Girishwar Misra later identified early Indian psychologists' tendency to apply Western tools without critically examining cultural fit — the "colonial psychology" problem.

1947 — Independence Era Begins

Psychology in India inherited a Western framework; the challenge of culturally relevant psychological science began to emerge.

  • Colonial Encounter: Indian psychology was established under British colonial rule, leading to uncritical adoption of Western frameworks that may not fit Indian social/cultural realities. Key figures: Ashis Nandy's critique of colonial psychology.

1.10 Major Schools of Psychology

📖 Definition

Structuralism (Wundt, Titchener) aimed to identify the basic elements (structures) of consciousness through trained introspection. Like chemistry seeks elements, structuralism sought the "atoms" of mind.

  • Method: Experimental introspection — trained observers reported their immediate conscious experiences
  • Elements: Sensations, images, feelings
  • E.B. Titchener brought structuralism to America; coined the term; distinguished sensation (quality, intensity, duration, clearness) from perception
  • Limitations: Introspection unreliable; ignored unconscious processes; could not study children/animals; static view of mind
📖 Definition

Functionalism (James, Dewey, Angell) studied the function (purpose) of mental processes in helping organisms adapt to their environment. Influenced by Darwin's evolutionary theory.

  • Mind as a flowing stream of consciousness (James), not static elements
  • Emphasized practical applications — education, mental health
  • Led to applied psychology, educational psychology, industrial psychology
  • John Dewey's reflex arc critique: behavior is holistic, not S-R chains
📖 Definition

Psychoanalysis (Freud) is a theory of personality, method of psychotherapy, and research method emphasizing the role of unconscious processes, childhood experiences, and internal conflicts in shaping behavior.

  • Topographic model: Conscious, Preconscious, Unconscious
  • Structural model: Id (pleasure principle), Ego (reality principle), Superego (morality)
  • Psychosexual stages: Oral, Anal, Phallic, Latency, Genital
  • Defense mechanisms: Repression, Denial, Projection, Rationalization, Sublimation, Displacement, Reaction formation
  • Techniques: Free association, dream analysis, transference analysis
📖 Definition

Gestalt Psychology (Wertheimer, Köhler, Koffka) emphasizes that psychological phenomena must be understood as organized, structured wholes rather than the sum of their parts. "The whole is more than the sum of its parts."

  • Founded: Max Wertheimer's phi phenomenon experiment (1912) — apparent motion from stationary stimuli
  • Köhler's insight learning (Sultan the chimp) — sudden problem-solving, not trial-and-error
  • Kurt Lewin's field theory — behavior = f(Person × Environment); B = f(P,E)
  • Laws of Perceptual Organization: Proximity, Similarity, Closure, Continuity, Figure-Ground
📖 Definition

Behaviorism (Watson, Skinner, Pavlov) holds that psychology should study only observable behavior, not subjective mental states. Behavior is determined by environmental stimuli and learned through conditioning.

  • Watson (1913): "Psychology as the Behaviorist Views It" — manifesto of behaviorism; S-R psychology
  • Classical Conditioning: Pavlov — neutral stimulus paired with unconditioned stimulus produces conditioned response
  • Operant Conditioning: Skinner — behavior shaped by consequences (reinforcement/punishment)
  • Little Albert: Watson's fear conditioning experiment — ethical issues
  • Radical Behaviorism: Skinner's view — even internal events (thoughts) are behaviors subject to learning principles
📖 Definition

Humanistic Psychology (Maslow, Rogers) focuses on human potential, self-actualization, free will, and the inherent worth and dignity of individuals. Sometimes called the "Third Force" against psychoanalysis and behaviorism.

  • Maslow's Hierarchy of Needs: Physiological → Safety → Love/Belonging → Esteem → Self-actualization (+ Transcendence)
  • Rogers' Person-Centered Approach: Unconditional positive regard, empathy, genuineness; actualizing tendency
  • Existential Psychology: (May, Yalom, Frankl) — confronts "givens" of existence: death, freedom, isolation, meaninglessness; Frankl's logotherapy
  • Phenomenology: Focus on lived experience as the primary source of knowledge
📖 Definition

Transpersonal Psychology (Maslow, Grof, Wilber) extends psychological inquiry beyond the individual self to include spiritual experiences, mystical states, and transcendent levels of consciousness. Called the "Fourth Force."

  • Incorporates Maslow's "peak experiences" and "B-values" (Being values)
  • Stanislav Grof's work on non-ordinary states of consciousness
  • Ken Wilber's Integral Theory (AQAL: All Quadrants, All Levels)
  • Incorporates insights from Eastern traditions (Buddhism, Vedanta, Sufism)
📖 Definition

The Cognitive Revolution (1950s–1970s) returned mental processes (attention, memory, reasoning, language) to the center of psychological inquiry, using the computer as a metaphor for mind. Led by Chomsky, Miller, Neisser, Simon, and Bruner.

  • Chomsky (1959): Critique of Skinner's Verbal Behavior — language requires innate grammatical structures
  • Miller (1956): "Magical number 7±2" — limits of working memory
  • Ulric Neisser (1967): Cognitive Psychology — landmark text; information processing approach
  • Information Processing Model: Input → Encoding → Storage → Retrieval → Output
  • Led to cognitive therapy (Aaron Beck), CBT, and modern neuroscience

Multicultural Psychology examines how cultural context shapes psychological processes, challenging the universality of Western psychological theories. Key aspects:

  • Emic vs. Etic distinction: Emic = culture-specific perspectives; Etic = cross-cultural universals
  • Critique of WEIRD psychology (Western, Educated, Industrialized, Rich, Democratic — Henrich et al., 2010)
  • Cultural psychology (Shweder, Cole) vs. cross-cultural psychology (Berry)
  • Individualism vs. Collectivism (Triandis, Hofstede)

1.11 Crisis in Psychology

The crisis in psychology refers to concerns about the discipline's overreliance on the experimental-analytical paradigm (logical empiricism) at the expense of holistic, qualitative, and experiential approaches.

  • Logical Empiricism: Only verifiable, observable phenomena count as scientific knowledge (Vienna Circle influence)
  • Replication Crisis: Many landmark findings in social psychology failed to replicate (Bem's ESP studies, ego depletion, priming effects)
  • Methodological critique: Over-reliance on laboratory experiments, student samples (WEIRD), self-report measures
  • Indic Influences on Modern Psychology: Mindfulness-based therapies, yoga therapy, meditation neuroscience (Davidson, Kabat-Zinn) — Eastern approaches now integrated into mainstream psychology

🔭 Research: Meaning, Purpose, and Dimensions

2.1 What is Psychological Research?

📖 Definition

Research is a systematic, empirical inquiry aimed at discovering, interpreting, and revising facts, events, behaviors, or theories. In psychology, it seeks to describe, explain, predict, and control behavior and mental processes.

Purposes of Research
  • Descriptive: Accurately characterizing a phenomenon (e.g., what is the prevalence of depression in college students?)
  • Explanatory: Identifying causes and mechanisms (e.g., does sleep deprivation cause impaired memory?)
  • Predictive: Forecasting future behavior (e.g., can SAT scores predict academic success?)
  • Applied: Solving practical problems (e.g., developing effective PTSD treatments)

Operational Definitions
📖 Definition

An operational definition specifies the exact procedures used to measure or manipulate a variable. It transforms abstract concepts into observable, measurable operations.

🌿 Example

"Anxiety" operationally defined as: scores on the Hamilton Anxiety Rating Scale (HAM-A) measured at intake. This makes the concept concrete and replicable.

Hypothesis
  • Research Hypothesis (H₁): A specific, testable prediction about the relationship between variables
  • Null Hypothesis (H₀): States no relationship exists; what statistical tests aim to reject
  • Directional Hypothesis: Specifies direction of effect (e.g., CBT group will have significantly lower depression than control)
  • Non-directional Hypothesis: States a difference exists but not its direction

2.3 Ethics in Research

⚠️ Critical for Exam

Ethics in psychological research is a high-priority exam topic. APA Ethics Code and Indian ethical guidelines are both relevant.

  • Informed Consent: Participants must be fully informed about nature, purpose, risks, and right to withdraw before participation
  • Voluntary Participation: No coercion; right to withdraw at any time without penalty
  • Confidentiality: Data identified only by codes; personal information protected
  • Anonymity: Researcher cannot link data to individual (stronger than confidentiality)
  • Deception: Permissible only when essential and study cannot be conducted otherwise; must be followed by full debriefing
  • Debriefing: Full disclosure after study; addressing any deception; ensuring participant wellbeing
  • Protection from Harm: Minimize physical and psychological risks; participant welfare paramount
  • Special populations: Extra protections for children, prisoners, individuals with mental illness
  • Institutional Review Board (IRB)/Ethical Committee: Mandatory review of research proposals
🌿 Historical Unethical Studies

Milgram Obedience Study (1963) — serious deception and distress without proper debriefing. Zimbardo's Stanford Prison Experiment (1971) — stopped early due to extreme psychological harm. Tuskegee Syphilis Study — withholding treatment from Black patients. These cases led to the Belmont Report (1979) establishing principles of research ethics.

📖 Definition

An experiment is the gold standard of psychological research: random assignment to conditions, manipulation of independent variable(s), control of extraneous variables, and measurement of dependent variables.

  • Random Assignment: Ensures groups are equivalent before manipulation; controls individual differences
  • Control Group: Receives no treatment or placebo; provides baseline for comparison
  • Placebo Control: Controls for expectation effects
  • Double-blind: Neither participants nor researchers know who received treatment — eliminates experimenter bias
  • Internal Validity: Confidence that IV caused DV change; threats include history, maturation, instrumentation, selection bias
  • External Validity: Generalizability of findings; threats include WEIRD samples, artificial lab settings

Quasi-experimental designs lack random assignment; used when randomization is impractical or unethical:

  • Pre-test–post-test without control group
  • Non-equivalent control group design
  • Interrupted time series design

2.7 Normal Probability Curve (NPC)

📖 Definition

The Normal Probability Curve (Bell Curve) is a symmetrical, unimodal distribution where mean = median = mode, and most scores cluster near the center, with progressively fewer scores toward the tails.

📐 Key Formulas

Z-score: z = (X − μ) / σ

Standard Normal: μ = 0, σ = 1

68-95-99.7 Rule: ±1σ → 68.26% | ±2σ → 95.44% | ±3σ → 99.74%

Properties: Symmetrical; Asymptotic tails; Mean = Median = Mode; Total area = 1.0

📐 Power Analysis & Effect Size

Statistical Power: Probability of correctly rejecting a false H₀ (avoiding Type II error β). Power = 1 − β. Target: ≥ 0.80

Effect Size (Cohen's d) = (M₁ − M₂) / SD_pooled

Cohen's benchmarks: d = 0.2 (small), d = 0.5 (medium), d = 0.8 (large)

Other effect sizes: η² (eta-squared) for ANOVA; r for correlations; ω² (omega-squared, less biased than η²)

G*Power: Standard software for power analysis; determines sample size needed

2.10 Regression Analysis

📐 Regression Formulas

Simple Linear Regression: Y = a + bX + ε

Where: Y = predicted criterion, a = intercept, b = regression coefficient (slope), X = predictor, ε = error

Multiple Regression: Y = a + b₁X₁ + b₂X₂ + ... + bₙXₙ + ε

Coefficient of Determination (R²): Proportion of variance in Y explained by predictor(s). R² × 100 = % variance explained.

Standardized Regression Coefficient (β): Effect of predictor in standard deviation units; used to compare predictors with different scales.

  • Assumptions of Regression: Linearity, Independence of errors, Normality of residuals, Homoscedasticity (equal variance), No multicollinearity (multiple regression)
  • Multicollinearity: When predictors are highly correlated — VIF > 10 indicates problem
  • Adjusted R²: Accounts for number of predictors; more honest than R² for multiple regression

🔷 Factor Analysis

2.11 Factor Analysis

📖 Definition

Factor Analysis (FA) is a statistical technique used to identify a smaller number of underlying latent variables (factors) that explain the pattern of correlations among a larger set of observed variables. Used extensively in scale development and personality research.

Types of Factor Analysis
  • Exploratory FA (EFA): Data-driven; no pre-specified factor structure; identifies underlying dimensions. Used in scale development.
  • Confirmatory FA (CFA): Theory-driven; tests whether data fit a pre-specified model. Used in construct validity assessment. Part of SEM.
Key Assumptions
  • Adequate sample size (N ≥ 200 or 10:1 ratio of cases to variables)
  • Interval/ratio data; approximately normal distributions
  • Variables must be intercorrelated (KMO ≥ 0.60; Bartlett's test of sphericity significant)
  • Linear relationships between variables
Methods of Extraction
  • Principal Components Analysis (PCA): Accounts for maximum variance; technically not FA but often used together
  • Principal Axis Factoring (PAF): Extracts shared variance (communalities); true FA method
  • Maximum Likelihood: Statistical model-based; provides chi-square test of fit
Rotation Methods
Rotation TypeMethodAssumptionBest Use
OrthogonalVarimax (maximize variance per factor); Quartimax; EquimaxFactors are uncorrelatedWhen factors are theoretically independent; simpler interpretation
ObliqueOblimin; Promax; GeominFactors are correlatedWhen factors are theoretically related (more realistic in psychology)
Interpretation
  • Factor Loading: Correlation of a variable with a factor. Loading ≥ 0.40 typically considered meaningful
  • Communality (h²): Proportion of variance in a variable explained by all factors together
  • Eigenvalue: Amount of variance explained by a factor. Retain factors with eigenvalue ≥ 1 (Kaiser criterion)
  • Scree Plot: Plot of eigenvalues; retain factors before the "elbow"
🌿 Example

Cattell's 16PF personality test emerged from factor analysis of hundreds of personality descriptors. Eysenck's PEN model (Psychoticism, Extraversion, Neuroticism) was derived through factor analysis. The Big Five (OCEAN) personality model is entirely factor-analytically derived.

📐 Item Analysis Formulas

Difficulty Index (p): p = Number passing / Total examinees. Optimal: 0.30–0.70. p=0.50 maximizes variability.

⚡ Neurons and Neurotransmitters

4.2 Neuron Structure

Neuron parts: Dendrites (receive input) → Cell Body/Soma (integrates signals) → Axon Hillock (generates action potential) → Axon (transmits) → Myelin Sheath (insulates; speeds conduction) → Nodes of Ranvier (saltatory conduction) → Axon Terminals/Synaptic Boutons (release neurotransmitters)

Types of Neurons
  • Sensory (Afferent): FROM receptors TO CNS; pseudounipolar
  • Motor (Efferent): FROM CNS TO muscles/glands; multipolar; large soma
  • Interneurons: Within CNS; most numerous (99%); association neurons
  • Bipolar: Retina, olfactory epithelium; one dendrite, one axon

4.3 Action Potential

📐 Action Potential Sequence

Resting Potential: −70 mV → Threshold: −55 mV → Depolarization (Na⁺ in) → Peak: +30 mV → Repolarization (K⁺ out) → Hyperpolarization → Resting.

All-or-none law: Once threshold reached, full action potential fires regardless of stimulus strength.

Saltatory conduction: AP "jumps" between nodes; myelinated fibers = 70–120 m/s vs. unmyelinated C-fibers = 0.5–2 m/s.

4.4 Synaptic Transmission Steps

  1. Action potential arrives at axon terminal
  2. Voltage-gated Ca²⁺ channels open
  3. Vesicles fuse with presynaptic membrane
  4. Neurotransmitters released into synaptic cleft (20–40 nm)
  5. NT binds to postsynaptic receptors → EPSP or IPSP
  6. NT removed: reuptake, enzymatic degradation, or diffusion

4.8 Neuroplasticity

📖 Definition

Neuroplasticity: Brain's ability to change structurally and functionally in response to experience throughout life.

5.6 Signal Detection Theory (SDT)

📖 Definition

SDT (Green & Swets, 1966) separates sensory sensitivity (d') from response bias (β/criterion c). Detection = signal strength + decision strategy.

Say "Yes"HIT (True Positive)FALSE ALARM (False Positive)
Say "No"MISS (False Negative)CORRECT REJECTION (True Negative)
  • d' (sensitivity): Distance between signal and noise distributions. Higher = better discrimination.
  • β (criterion/bias): Conservative (fewer false alarms, also fewer hits) vs. Liberal (many hits, also many false alarms)
  • ROC Curve: Plots hit rate vs. false alarm rate; AUC = overall sensitivity

💛 Emotion

7.7 Physiological Correlates of Emotion

  • Autonomic NS activation: Fear/anger → sympathetic activation (HR↑, BP↑, sweating, pupil dilation, digestion suppressed)
  • Polygraph (Lie Detector): Measures physiological arousal (GSR, HR, BP, respiration) as proxies for emotion; validity disputed

7.9 Emotion Regulation

  • Gross's Process Model: Situation Selection → Situation Modification → Attentional Deployment → Cognitive Change (Reappraisal) → Response Modulation (Suppression). Antecedent-focused (reappraisal) more adaptive than response-focused (suppression).
  • Cognitive Reappraisal: Reinterpreting meaning of emotional stimulus — reduces emotional intensity; does not impair memory; socially beneficial
  • Expressive Suppression: Inhibiting emotional expression while feeling emotion internally — maintains arousal; impairs memory; socially isolating
  • Mindfulness-Based Regulation: Non-judgmental awareness of emotions without suppression or rumination; DBT's emotion regulation module

👥 Nature, Scope, and History of Social Psychology

8.1 Definition and Key Concepts

📖 Definition

Social Psychology (Allport, 1954) is "the scientific study of how the actual, imagined, or implied presence of other people affects the thoughts, feelings, and behaviors of individuals."

Key milestones: Triplett (1898) — first social psychology experiment (social facilitation via cycling); McDougall & Ross published first social psychology texts (1908); Kurt Lewin — "nothing so practical as a good theory"; field theory; action research; Floyd Allport — experimental emphasis; crisis of the 1970s (ethical concerns; replication); cognitive revolution in social psych (social cognition).

💬 Social Perception, Attitudes, and Prosocial Behavior

8.3 Communication and Attribution

  • Social Perception: How we form impressions of others; Central traits (Asch's warm/cold study); Primacy vs. Recency effects; Halo effect (one trait biases overall judgment)
  • Attribution Theory (Heider, 1958): Naive psychology; perceived causes of behavior — Internal (dispositional) vs. External (situational)
  • Kelley's Covariation Model: Three criteria: Consistency (does it happen repeatedly?), Distinctiveness (unique to this entity?), Consensus (do others respond similarly?). High consistency + low distinctiveness + low consensus → Internal attribution
  • Fundamental Attribution Error (FAE)/Correspondence Bias: Over-attributing others' behavior to disposition while ignoring situational factors; Ross (1977); quiz master study
  • Actor-Observer Bias: Attribute own behavior to situation; others' behavior to disposition
  • Self-Serving Bias: Attribute success to internal factors; failure to external factors; protects self-esteem
  • Non-verbal Communication: Mehrabian's 7-38-55 rule (7% words, 38% tone, 55% body language); though disputed in context; proxemics (Hall's zones); kinesics; eye contact

8.4 Attitude Formation and Change

📖 Definition

An attitude is a relatively enduring evaluation (positive or negative) of an object, person, group, or event. Three components: ABC — Affective (emotional), Behavioral (predisposition to act), Cognitive (beliefs).

  • Attitude Formation: Classical conditioning (mere exposure effect — Zajonc; familiarity → liking), Operant conditioning (reinforcement of expressions), Observational learning, Social comparison
  • Mere Exposure Effect (Zajonc, 1968): Repeated exposure to stimulus increases liking — even without awareness; operates subliminally
  • Attitude-Behavior Consistency: Low correlations (LaPiere study, 1934); moderated by attitude specificity, strength, accessibility, vested interest; Theory of Planned Behavior (Ajzen) — intention mediates attitude-behavior link
  • Elaboration Likelihood Model (ELM, Petty & Cacioppo): Central route (careful argument processing; strong attitude change; motivation + ability) vs. Peripheral route (surface cues — attractiveness, expertise; weaker, less durable change)
  • Cognitive Dissonance (Festinger): Acting against attitudes → attitude change to reduce dissonance; insufficient justification; post-decisional dissonance
  • Cultural Context: Individualistic cultures → personal attitude emphasized; Collectivistic cultures → group norms shape attitudes more; cross-cultural attitude research (Triandis)

8.5 Prosocial Behavior

  • Bystander Effect (Latané & Darley, 1968): Presence of others DECREASES helping; triggered by Kitty Genovese murder; diffusion of responsibility; pluralistic ignorance; evaluation apprehension; step model of helping: Notice → Interpret as emergency → Assume responsibility → Know how to help → Implement help
  • Altruism: Selfless helping; Batson's empathy-altruism hypothesis: empathy → genuinely altruistic motivation (not to reduce own distress)
  • Social Exchange Theory: Helping analyzed as cost-benefit; help when benefits exceed costs
  • Kin Selection: More likely to help genetic relatives; Hamilton's inclusive fitness
  • Reciprocal Altruism (Trivers): Help non-kin if reciprocation likely in future

8.7 Aggression

  • Frustration-Aggression Hypothesis (Dollard et al., 1939): Frustration always leads to some form of aggression; aggression always caused by frustration — modified by Berkowitz (1989): frustration → negative affect → aggression if aggressive cues present
  • Social Learning Theory (Bandura): Aggression learned through observation and reinforcement; Bobo doll studies; media violence effects
  • Excitation Transfer (Zillmann): Residual arousal from one situation (exercise) misattributed to provocative situation → amplified aggression
  • Cognitive Neoassociation Model (Berkowitz): Aversive events → negative affect → activates fight-or-flight networks → aggression or fear depending on cues
  • General Aggression Model (Anderson & Bushman): Integrative model; person (traits, hostile attributions) + situation (weapons, frustration, provocation) → arousal/affect/cognition → aggression

🔗 Group Dynamics, Leadership, and Intergroup Relations

8.8 Group Dynamics

  • Group: Two or more people with shared goals, mutual interdependence, interaction, and a sense of belonging
  • Group Cohesion: "We-ness"; attraction to group and fellow members; increases conformity, performance (for valued tasks), social support
  • Group Polarization: After discussion, group decisions become more extreme than individual pre-discussion positions — risky shift or cautious shift depending on initial tendency
  • Groupthink (Janis, 1972): Cohesive group's excessive agreement-seeking overrides realistic appraisal; illusion of invulnerability, collective rationalization, self-censorship, pressure on dissenters; Bay of Pigs, Challenger disaster examples; prevention: devil's advocate, outside expert, anonymous survey
  • Deindividuation: Loss of individual identity in crowds → reduced self-monitoring → disinhibited behavior; uniform/anonymity effects (Zimbardo); reduced accountability

  • Private Speech → Inner Speech: Overt self-talk becomes internalized; tool for self-regulation
  • Cultural Tools: Language, number systems, writing — mediate cognitive development
  • Contrast with Piaget: Vygotsky emphasizes social and cultural context; ZPD vs. Piaget's stage readiness

⚖️ Gender, Poverty, Disability, and Migration

10.1 Cultural Bias and Discrimination

  • Gender: Social construction vs. biological sex; gender identity, gender expression, sexual orientation (distinct dimensions); gender bias in psychology (Bem's androgyny; Gilligan's critique of Kohlberg); intersectionality (Crenshaw) — overlapping social identities create unique experiences of discrimination
  • Stereotype Threat (Steele & Aronson, 1995): Awareness of negative stereotype about one's group impairs performance on relevant tasks; mediates group differences in test scores; reduced by self-affirmation, role models, growth mindset
  • Poverty: Absolute poverty (below survival threshold) vs. Relative poverty (below societal standard); Cognitive tax theory (Mullainathan & Shafir) — scarcity reduces cognitive bandwidth; poverty → learned helplessness; adverse childhood experiences (ACEs)
  • Disability: Medical model (problem in individual) vs. Social model (barriers created by society); psychological adjustment to disability; identity-first vs. person-first language; stigma, discrimination, ableism
  • Migration: Acculturative stress (Berry's four acculturation strategies: Integration, Assimilation, Separation, Marginalization); Berry's framework; bicultural identity; cultural bereavement

10.2 Stigma, Marginalization, and Social Suffering

  • Stigma (Goffman, 1963): Spoiled identity; three types: Abominations of body (physical), Blemishes of character (mental illness, addiction), Tribal stigma (race, nationality, religion); courtesy stigma (by association)
  • Social Suffering (Kleinman, Das & Lock): Human suffering caused and experienced through the social world; intersections of political violence, poverty, oppression with personal experience of pain
  • Child Abuse and Domestic Violence: Physical, emotional, sexual, neglect; intergenerational transmission; cycle of violence hypothesis; trauma-informed care; mandated reporting; PTSD and complex PTSD (C-PTSD — Herman)

☮️ Peace Psychology

10.3 Violence, Non-violence, and Conflict Resolution

📖 Definition

Peace Psychology (Christie, Wagner & Winter) examines psychological factors in violence, war, and oppression; applies psychological knowledge to promote nonviolent conflict resolution, reconciliation, and cultures of peace at macro levels.

  • Types of Violence: Direct/physical violence (overt harm), Structural violence (Galtung) — injustice embedded in social systems; Cultural violence — legitimizes structural and direct violence
  • Positive vs. Negative Peace (Galtung): Negative peace = absence of direct violence; Positive peace = presence of social justice, cooperation, equality — the goal of peace psychology
  • Nonviolence: Gandhi's satyagraha (truth-force); King's civil rights movement; psychological principles: courage, nonretaliation, dehumanization prevention
  • Macro-level Conflict Resolution: Truth and Reconciliation Commissions (South Africa); Restorative Justice; Intergroup Contact Theory (Allport's conditions: equal status, common goals, institutional support, cooperative contact)
  • Role of Media: Media can escalate conflict (enemy images, dehumanization) or promote peace (peace journalism — Galtung); social media amplification of outrage; counter-narratives; media literacy

🌟 Wellbeing and Self-Growth

10.4 Types of Wellbeing

📖 Hedonic vs. Eudaimonic Wellbeing

Hedonic wellbeing: Subjective wellbeing (SWB) = high Positive Affect + low Negative Affect + high Life Satisfaction (Diener's model). Pleasure-based; happiness and absence of pain.

Eudaimonic wellbeing: Psychological wellbeing (PWB) = realizing one's potential, meaningful engagement, virtue. Ryff's six dimensions: Autonomy, Environmental Mastery, Personal Growth, Positive Relations, Purpose in Life, Self-Acceptance. Flourishing = both hedonic + eudaimonic (Seligman's PERMA model).

10.5 Character Strengths

  • VIA Classification (Peterson & Seligman, 2004): Values in Action — 24 character strengths organized under 6 virtues: Wisdom, Courage, Humanity, Justice, Temperance, Transcendence
  • Signature Strengths: Top personal strengths; using them regularly → increased wellbeing and engagement; strengths-based interventions show lasting effects
  • PERMA Model (Seligman, 2011): Positive emotions + Engagement + Relationships + Meaning + Accomplishment = complete wellbeing framework

10.6 Resilience and Post-Traumatic Growth

  • Resilience: Bouncing back from adversity; dynamic process, not fixed trait; protective factors: secure attachment, self-efficacy, social support, meaning-making; Masten's "ordinary magic" — resilience is common, not extraordinary
  • Post-Traumatic Growth (PTG): Calhoun & Tedeschi (1996) — positive psychological change from highly challenging life circumstances; five domains: New Possibilities, Personal Strength, Relating to Others, Appreciation for Life, Spiritual Change/Existential Understanding
  • Distinction from Resilience: Resilience = returning to baseline; PTG = going BEYOND previous functioning; not mutually exclusive

10.9 Psychoneuroimmunology (PNI)

📖 Definition

Psychoneuroimmunology (Ader & Cohen, 1975) studies bidirectional interactions between psychological processes, nervous system, and immune system. Coined from: Psycho (mind) + Neuro (nervous system) + Immunology (immune function).

  • Key Findings: Stress → HPA activation → cortisol → immunosuppression; chronic stress → increased susceptibility to infection; bereavement → lymphocyte depression; social support → enhanced NK cell activity; wound healing slower under exam stress (Kiecolt-Glaser)
  • Cancer: Psychosocial factors in cancer onset (Type C behavior; stress → tumor progression via hormonal/immune pathways); psycho-oncology; acceptance-based interventions; meaning-centered psychotherapy (Breitbart)
  • HIV/AIDS: Psychological factors affect disease progression; depression → faster HIV progression; stigma reduces help-seeking; cognitive-behavioral stress management (CBSM — Ironson) improves immune function; disclosure and social support protective

💻 Psychology and Technology Interface

10.10 Digital Psychology

  • Digital Learning: E-learning platforms; MOOCs; gamification; spaced repetition systems (SRS); adaptive learning; virtual reality (VR) simulations; benefits for access; risks of passive consumption; digital fatigue; attention spans
  • Digital Etiquette (Netiquette): Online communication norms; respectful discourse; anonymity effects (online disinhibition effect — Suler); virtual identity management; professional vs. personal online presence
  • Cyber Bullying: Deliberate, repeated harm through digital devices; characteristics: anonymity, broad audience, 24/7 exposure, permanence; types: harassment, flaming, exclusion, outing, impersonation, stalking; psychological consequences: depression, anxiety, suicidality; intervention: reporting mechanisms, bystander intervention, digital literacy programs
  • Cyber Pornography: Online pornography consumption; implications: normalization of sexual aggression, unrealistic sexual expectations, potential addiction (compulsive sexual behavior); adolescent exposure; neurobiological parallels with substance addiction; dopamine reward pathways; impact on relationships (Gottman research)
  • Parental Mediation of Digital Usage: Three strategies: Active mediation (co-viewing, discussion), Restrictive mediation (rules, screen time limits), Technical mediation (parental controls, filters); AAP guidelines (screen time recommendations by age); digital literacy parenting; COPPA regulations
  • Internet Addiction: Problematic internet use; Young's diagnostic criteria; subtypes: gaming disorder (recognized by ICD-11), social media addiction, compulsive online shopping; treatment: CBT, motivational interviewing, digital detox
  • Smartphone and Social Media Effects: FOMO (Fear of Missing Out); social comparison; depression and anxiety correlation (Twenge's research on iGen); benefits: social connection, access to information; Dunbar's number and online social networks
