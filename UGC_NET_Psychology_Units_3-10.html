Unit 3: Psychological Testing

Types of Psychological Tests

Type Purpose Characteristics Examples
Achievement Tests Measure learned knowledge/skills Subject-specific; curriculum-based Exam scores, proficiency tests
Aptitude Tests Predict future performance Future-oriented; ability-based SAT, GRE, CAT
Intelligence Tests Measure cognitive abilities General mental ability; IQ Wechsler scales, Stanford-Binet
Personality Tests Assess personality traits/disorders Subjective; interpretive MMPI-2, Big Five, MBTI
Projective Tests Reveal unconscious processes Ambiguous stimuli; subjective Rorschach, TAT
Interest Inventories Assess vocational interests Career-oriented; preference-based Strong Interest Inventory, KUDER
Neuropsychological Tests Assess brain-behavior relationships Diagnostic; clinical Wisconsin Card Sorting, Halstead-Reitan
Creativity Tests Measure creative thinking Divergent thinking; novelty Torrance Tests of Creative Thinking
Frequently Asked Question Distinguish between objective and projective personality tests. Objective tests use structured responses while projective tests use ambiguous stimuli interpreted by examiner.

Test Construction and Standardization

Item Writing
  • Clear, unambiguous language
  • Appropriate difficulty level
  • Single concept per item
  • Avoid biased or discriminatory language
  • Multiple-choice format: clear correct answer
Item Analysis
  • Difficulty Index: Proportion answering correctly (0-1)
  • Discrimination Index: Ability to distinguish high from low performers
  • Distractors: Plausible but incorrect options
  • Item-Total Correlation: Item relation to total score
  • Items with poor statistics may be revised or eliminated
Test Standardization
  • Consistent administration procedures
  • Uniform testing environment
  • Standard instructions and timing
  • Enables score comparison across individuals
Reliability Consistency of test measurement:
  • Test-Retest: Stability over time; r > 0.70
  • Internal Consistency: Item intercorrelation; Cronbach's alpha > 0.70
  • Inter-rater Reliability: Agreement between raters; Cohen's kappa
  • Parallel Forms: Equivalent versions produce similar scores
Validity Degree test measures what it claims to measure:
  • Content Validity: Represents content domain adequately
  • Criterion Validity: Predicts relevant outcomes
  • Construct Validity: Measures theoretical construct
  • Convergent Validity: Correlates with related measures
  • Discriminant Validity: Doesn't correlate with unrelated measures
Norms
  • Reference group scores for interpretation
  • Percentile ranks, standard scores, scaled scores
  • Allow individual score interpretation
  • Should be representative and current
Validity Types: Remember CCDC
  • Content validity
  • Criterion validity
  • Discriminant validity
  • Construct validity (also Convergent)

Attitude Scales

Semantic Differential
  • Measures attitude on bipolar scales
  • Example: Good—Bad, Strong—Weak
  • 7-point scale typical
  • Rapid, easy to administer
  • Useful for evaluating concepts
Stapel Scale
  • Single adjective with +5 to -5 rating
  • Measures both direction and intensity
  • Less commonly used than others
Likert Scale
  • Agreement scale (Strongly Agree to Strongly Disagree)
  • Usually 5-point: SA, A, Neutral, D, SD
  • Most widely used for attitudes
  • Easy interpretation; reliable
  • Example: "Psychology is fascinating" - rate agreement
Example: Likert Scale for Studying Psychology
  • 1 = Strongly Disagree
  • 2 = Disagree
  • 3 = Neutral
  • 4 = Agree
  • 5 = Strongly Agree
  • Item: "I enjoy learning psychology concepts"

Unit 4: Biological Basis of Behavior

Sensory Systems and Neurons

Sensory Systems
  • General Sensations: Touch, temperature, pain - from skin and internal organs
  • Specific Sensations: Vision, hearing, taste, smell, balance
  • Receptors: Specialized cells detecting stimuli
  • Sensory Processes: Transduction (stimulus to neural signal), transmission, perception
Neurons: Structure and Function
  • Soma (Cell Body): Contains nucleus; metabolic center
  • Dendrites: Receive signals; tree-like; multiple per neuron
  • Axon: Transmits signals; single; may have branches (axon terminals)
  • Axon Hillock: Triggers action potential
  • Synapse: Gap between neurons
Neuron Types
  • Sensory (Afferent): Receptor to CNS
  • Motor (Efferent): CNS to muscle/gland
  • Interneurons: Connect neurons within CNS
Neural Impulse/Action Potential
  • Resting Potential: -70 mV (inside negative relative to outside)
  • Depolarization: Membrane potential becomes less negative; reaches threshold (-55 mV)
  • Action Potential: Rapid change in membrane potential; all-or-nothing
  • Phases: Depolarization (sodium influx), Repolarization (potassium efflux), Hyperpolarization
  • Refractory Period: Absolute (no new AP) and relative (higher threshold)
  • Propagation: Along axon; faster with myelin (saltatory conduction)
Synaptic Transmission
  • Presynaptic Neuron: Transmitting neuron
  • Synaptic Cleft: Gap (20 nm)
  • Postsynaptic Neuron: Receiving neuron
  • Process: AP arrives → Ca²+ influx → vesicle release → transmitter diffusion → receptor binding
  • Excitatory Postsynaptic Potential (EPSC): Depolarizing; increases firing probability
  • Inhibitory Postsynaptic Potential (IPSC): Hyperpolarizing; decreases firing probability
  • Integration: Sum of EPSCs and IPSCs determines postsynaptic response
Neurotransmitters Chemical messengers for interneuronal communication:
  • Acetylcholine (ACh): Memory, attention, motor control; autonomic; neuromuscular
  • Dopamine: Reward, motivation, motor control, attention; depleted in Parkinson's
  • Serotonin: Mood, sleep, appetite; deficiency in depression
  • Norepinephrine: Arousal, attention, stress response
  • GABA: Main inhibitory; anxiety reduction; affected by benzodiazepines
  • Glutamate: Main excitatory; learning, memory
  • Endorphins: Opioid system; pain relief, reward
Major Neurotransmitters: DANCE GABS
  • Dopamine
  • Acetylcholine
  • Norepinephrine
  • Cortisol
  • Endorphins
  • Glutamate
  • Adrenergic
  • Benzos (GABA)
  • Serotonin

Nervous System Organization

Central Nervous System (CNS) Brain and spinal cord:
  • Brain: Cerebrum, cerebellum, brainstem, limbic structures
  • Spinal Cord: Extends from medulla to lumbar region; relays signals; reflex center
Peripheral Nervous System (PNS)
  • Somatic Nervous System: Voluntary, skeletal muscles; sensory input
  • Autonomic Nervous System: Involuntary, smooth muscle, glands
Autonomic Nervous System
  • Sympathetic: "Fight or flight"; increases HR, BP, pupil dilation; norepinephrine
  • Parasympathetic: "Rest and digest"; decreases HR, BP; promotes digestion; acetylcholine
  • Enteric: "Gut brain"; controls GI functioning; semiautonomous
Neuroplasticity Nervous system's ability to change structure/function:
  • Structural Plasticity: Brain regions change function (compensation after injury)
  • Functional Plasticity: Different regions assume lost function
  • Synaptic Plasticity: Strength of synapses changes with activity (LTP, LTD)
  • Critical Periods: Heightened plasticity early in development
  • Experience-Dependent: Learning strengthens neural connections
  • Examples: London taxi drivers with enlarged hippocampus; phantom limb sensation

Methods of Physiological Psychology

Invasive Methods (Direct Brain Access)
  • Anatomical Methods: Brain dissection, histology
  • Lesion Techniques: Remove or damage brain regions; observe behavioral changes
  • Degeneration Techniques: Study neural degeneration after lesion
  • Chemical Methods: Inject drugs/chemicals affecting specific neurotransmitters
  • Microelectrode Studies: Single-cell recording; measure neuronal activity
Non-invasive Methods
  • EEG (Electroencephalography): Scalp electrodes record electrical activity; cheap, temporal resolution; poor spatial resolution
  • fMRI (Functional MRI): Blood oxygen changes; good spatial resolution; expensive
  • PET (Positron Emission Tomography): Radioactive tracers; metabolic activity
  • MEG (Magnetoencephalography): Magnetic field changes; excellent temporal resolution
  • CT (Computed Tomography): Structural imaging; good for tumors/damage
  • MRI (Magnetic Resonance Imaging): Detailed structural images; no radiation

Biological Basis of Motivation and Emotion

Biological Basis of Motivation
  • Hunger: Lateral hypothalamus (feeding center); ventromedial hypothalamus (satiety center); glucose, leptin, ghrelin
  • Thirst: Osmoreceptors detect dehydration; hypothalamus; angiotensin II
  • Sleep: Suprachiasmatic nucleus controls circadian rhythm; reticular activating system; neurotransmitters (serotonin)
  • Sex: Hormones (testosterone, estrogen); hypothalamus; dopamine; sensory stimuli
Biological Basis of Emotion
  • Limbic System: Amygdala (fear), hippocampus (memory), hypothalamus (hormone release)
  • Amygdala: Processes emotional significance; fear conditioning; aggression
  • Prefrontal Cortex: Emotion regulation; fear extinction
  • Hormonal Regulation: Cortisol (stress), epinephrine (arousal), oxytocin (bonding)
Genetics and Behavior
  • Chromosomal Anomalies: Down syndrome (trisomy 21), Turner (XO), Klinefelter (XXY)
  • Nature-Nurture Controversy: Genetic vs. environmental influences
  • Twin Studies: Monozygotic (identical) vs. dizygotic (fraternal); heritability estimates
  • Adoption Studies: Separate genetic and environmental factors
  • Molecular Genetics: Specific genes influencing behavior (COMT, MAOA, 5-HTTLPR)
Remember Heritability = Genetic variance / Total variance; varies by trait and environment

Unit 5: Attention, Perception, Learning, Memory and Forgetting

Attention

Forms of Attention
  • Sustained Attention: Maintaining focus over time; vigilance
  • Selective Attention: Focusing on relevant stimuli while ignoring others (cocktail party effect)
  • Divided Attention: Attending to multiple stimuli simultaneously; limited capacity
  • Alternating Attention: Switching focus between tasks
Models of Attention
  • Broadbent's Filter Theory: Early selection; bottleneck; filters irrelevant info early
  • Treisman's Attenuation Model: Attenuates (weakens) unattended information; semantic processing
  • Deutsch-Norman Late Selection: Selection occurs late; all stimuli processed semantically
  • Capacity Model (Kahneman): Attention as resource; limited capacity

Perception

Approaches to Perception
  • Gestalt Approach: Whole greater than parts; organization principles
  • Physiological Approach: Sensory receptors and neural mechanisms
Perceptual Organization: Gestalt Principles
  • Figure and Ground: Distinguish foreground from background
  • Proximity: Close stimuli grouped together
  • Similarity: Similar stimuli grouped together
  • Continuity: Smooth, continuous patterns preferred
  • Closure: Complete incomplete figures mentally
  • Common Fate: Objects moving similarly grouped together
Perceptual Constancy Perceiving objects as constant despite changing sensory input:
  • Size Constancy: Object size constant despite distance
  • Shape Constancy: Object shape constant despite angle
  • Color Constancy: Object color constant despite lighting
  • Lightness Constancy: Object brightness constant despite illumination
Depth Perception
  • Binocular Cues: Use both eyes; stereopsis; retinal disparity
  • Monocular Cues: One eye sufficient; linear perspective, relative size, occlusion
  • Motion Parallax: Objects move different speeds; indicates depth
Signal Detection Theory
  • Hit: Detect signal when present
  • Miss: Fail to detect signal when present
  • False Alarm: Report signal when absent
  • Correct Rejection: Correctly report no signal
  • d': Sensitivity measure; ability to detect signal
  • Criterion: Decision threshold; affects hits and false alarms
Role of Motivation and Learning in Perception
  • Hungry individuals perceive food-related stimuli faster (need-based perception)
  • Expert musicians perceive musical patterns differently (expertise effect)
  • Experience shapes perceptual expectations (top-down processing)
  • Cultural experience influences perceptual interpretation
Key Distinction Sensation (sensory input) vs. Perception (interpretation); Subliminal perception occurs below conscious threshold.

Learning

Fundamental Theories of Learning
  • Thorndike's Law of Effect: Behaviors followed by satisfaction strengthened; unpleasant consequences weakened
  • Guthrie's Contiguity Theory: Pairing of stimulus and response; last response before stimulus ceases is learned
  • Hull's Drive-Reduction Theory: Learning reduces drives/needs; motivation for learning
Classical Conditioning
  • Unconditioned Stimulus (US): Naturally elicits response
  • Unconditioned Response (UR): Natural response to US
  • Conditioned Stimulus (CS): Initially neutral; paired with US
  • Conditioned Response (CR): Learned response to CS
  • Acquisition: CS-US pairing strengthens association
  • Extinction: CR diminishes when CS presented without US
  • Spontaneous Recovery: CR reappears after extinction
  • Stimulus Generalization: CR occurs to similar stimuli
  • Stimulus Discrimination: CR occurs only to trained stimulus
Instrumental Learning (Operant Conditioning)
  • Thorndike's Puzzle Box: Animals learn to escape for reward
  • Skinner's Operant Conditioning: Reinforcement strengthens; punishment weakens
  • Reinforcement: Increases behavior frequency
  • Positive Reinforcement: Add desirable consequence
  • Negative Reinforcement: Remove undesirable consequence
  • Punishment: Decreases behavior frequency
  • Positive Punishment: Add undesirable consequence
  • Negative Punishment: Remove desirable consequence
Reinforcement Schedules
  • Continuous: Every response reinforced; quick acquisition, quick extinction
  • Fixed Ratio: Reinforcement after N responses; high rate (e.g., piece-work pay)
  • Variable Ratio: Reinforcement after variable responses; highest rate, resistant to extinction (slot machines)
  • Fixed Interval: Reinforcement after time period; scallop pattern (post office model)
  • Variable Interval: Reinforcement after variable time periods; steady rate, resistant to extinction (pop quizzes)
Behavior Modification
  • Systematic application of learning principles to change behavior
  • Token economies; contingency contracting; extinction procedures
  • Applications: clinical, educational, organizational settings
Cognitive Approaches to Learning
  • Latent Learning: Learning occurs without reinforcement; not demonstrated until needed
  • Cognitive Maps: Mental representations of environment; Tolman's research
  • Observational Learning: Learning through observation (modeling/vicarious learning); Bandura
  • Self-Efficacy: Belief in ability to succeed; determines motivation and persistence
Verbal and Discrimination Learning
  • Paired-associate learning; serial learning
  • Learning to distinguish between similar stimuli
  • Overlearning and transfer effects
Neurophysiology of Learning
  • Long-Term Potentiation (LTP): Synaptic strengthening with repeated stimulation
  • Long-Term Depression (LTD): Synaptic weakening
  • Synaptic Consolidation: Protein synthesis; memory trace development
  • Hippocampus: Critical for memory formation
Reinforcement Schedules: Remember VR-VI or CRF
  • FRame - Fixed Ratio (work like pigeons)
  • VR - Variable Ratio (most resistant, gambling)
  • FI - Fixed Interval (post office model)
  • VI - Variable Interval (pop quizzes, steady)
  • CRF - Continuous Reinforcement (easiest learning, fastest extinction)

Memory and Forgetting

Memory Processes
  • Encoding: Converting information into memory representation
  • Storage: Maintaining information over time
  • Retrieval: Accessing stored information
Stages of Memory
  • Sensory Memory: Iconic (visual 0.5s), echoic (auditory 2-3s); very brief; capacity high
  • Short-Term/Working Memory: 7±2 items; ~20 seconds; active processing; capacity limited
  • Long-Term Memory: Relatively permanent; very large capacity; organized
Types of Long-Term Memory
  • Declarative (Explicit): Conscious recall; can be verbalized
  • Episodic: Events and experiences; "What" memory
  • Semantic: Facts and concepts; general knowledge
  • Procedural (Implicit): Skills and habits; automatic; cannot easily verbalize
Theories of Forgetting
  • Decay Theory: Memory fades with time; disuse
  • Interference Theory: Similar memories interfere; proactive (old interferes new) and retroactive (new interferes old)
  • Retrieval Failure Theory: Information stored but retrieval cues unavailable
  • Motivated Forgetting (Repression): Psychoanalytic theory; emotionally threatening memories suppressed
Key Point The serial position effect: primacy effect (first items remembered better) and recency effect (recent items remembered better).

Unit 6: Thinking, Intelligence and Creativity

Intelligence Theories

Theorist Theory Key Components
Spearman Two-Factor Theory (g + s) General intelligence (g) + specific abilities (s)
Thurstone Primary Mental Abilities Seven independent abilities (verbal, spatial, reasoning, etc.)
Cattell Fluid & Crystallized Intelligence Fluid (abstract reasoning, flexibility); Crystallized (knowledge, experience)
Jensen Hierarchical Model General intelligence at top; group factors; specific abilities
Gardner Multiple Intelligences 9 types: linguistic, logical-math, spatial, bodily-kinesthetic, musical, interpersonal, intrapersonal, naturalistic, existential
Sternberg Triarchic Theory Analytical (problem-solving), creative, practical intelligence
Goleman Emotional Intelligence Self-awareness, self-regulation, motivation, empathy, social skills
Das, Kar & Parrila PASS Model Planning, Attention, Simultaneous, Sequential processing
Creativity Theorists
  • Torrance: Divergent thinking; fluency, flexibility, originality, elaboration
  • Getzels & Jackson: Creative vs. intelligent individuals; different paths to achievement
  • Guilford: Structure of Intellect; 150 intellectual factors; divergent production
  • Wallach & Kogan: Creativity as personality trait; tolerance for ambiguity
Intelligence-Creativity Relationship
  • Moderate positive correlation
  • Threshold theory: Minimum IQ necessary but not sufficient for creativity
  • Different cognitive styles; intelligence ≠ creativity
  • Both important for achievement

Unit 7: Personality, Motivation, Emotion, Stress and Coping

Personality Approaches

Approach Focus Key Concepts
Psychoanalytic (Freud) Unconscious drives Psychosexual stages, id-ego-superego, defense mechanisms
Neo-Freudian Social factors Jung (collective unconscious), Adler (inferiority), Erikson (stages)
Social Learning Environment Bandura - self-efficacy, observational learning, reciprocal determinism
Trait Stable characteristics Big Five: Openness, Conscientiousness, Extraversion, Agreeableness, Neuroticism
Humanistic Self-actualization Rogers - congruence, unconditional positive regard; Maslow - self-actualization
Cognitive Thought patterns Beck - automatic thoughts affect personality and behavior
Existential Meaning and freedom Authenticity, responsibility, existential anxiety
Transpersonal Spiritual dimensions Transcendence, higher consciousness, spiritual experiences
Other Important Theories
  • Rotter's Locus of Control: Internal vs. external attributions for events
  • Seligman's Explanatory Style: Permanent vs. temporary, global vs. specific, internal vs. external
  • Kohlberg's Moral Development: Pre-conventional, conventional, post-conventional stages

Motivation

Basic Motivational Concepts
  • Instincts: Innate biological drives; McDougall's theory
  • Needs: Psychological requirements; Maslow's hierarchy
  • Drives: Internal states prompting action
  • Arousal: Physiological activation level
  • Incentives: External stimuli motivating behavior
  • Motivational Cycle: Need → Drive → Goal-directed behavior → Drive reduction
Approaches to Motivation
  • Psychoanalytic: Unconscious drives (Freud); libidinous energy
  • Ethological: Instinctive behaviors; evolutionary basis
  • S-R (Behavioral): Conditioning and reinforcement
  • Cognitive: Goals, expectations, attributions
  • Humanistic: Self-actualization, personal growth
Social Motives
  • Achievement Motivation (McClelland): Need to succeed; hope of success vs. fear of failure
  • Affiliation Motivation: Need for social connection; influenced by anxiety
  • Power Motivation: Need to influence others; leadership
  • Motivational Competence: Beliefs about ability to achieve goals
  • Self-Regulation: Managing motivation and impulses
  • Flow (Csikszentmihalyi): Optimal engagement when challenge matches skill

Emotion and Stress Management

Emotion Theories
  • James-Lange: Physiological changes → emotion ("feel scared because running")
  • Cannon-Bard: Simultaneous physiological and emotional responses
  • Schachter-Singer: Arousal + cognition = emotion
  • Lazarus: Appraisal of stimulus → emotion
  • Lindsley: Reticular activating system and emotion
Emotion Regulation
  • Cognitive reappraisal; expressive suppression
  • Meditation and mindfulness; breathing exercises
  • Social support; coping strategies
Stress Management Strategies
  • Biofeedback: Monitor and control physiological responses
  • Music Therapy: Calming effect; stress reduction
  • Breathing Exercises: Deep breathing; diaphragmatic breathing
  • Progressive Muscular Relaxation: Tense then relax muscles
  • Guided Imagery: Visualize calming scenes
  • Mindfulness: Non-judgmental present awareness
  • Meditation: Various forms; regular practice
  • Yogasana: Physical postures; flexibility and strength
  • Stress Inoculation Training: Gradual exposure to manage stress responses
Type A, B, C, D Behaviors
  • Type A: Competitive, aggressive, time-urgent (heart disease risk)
  • Type B: Relaxed, easygoing, patient
  • Type C: Suppressed emotions, cooperative (cancer risk)
  • Type D: Negative affect, social inhibition (distressed)
