PREFACE

A Brief History of Artificial Intelligence

Understanding where we came from illuminates where we are going. The story of AI is one of grand ambitions, painful winters, and stunning resurgences.

1943
McCulloch & Pitts — The First Neuron Model
Warren McCulloch and Walter Pitts published the first mathematical model of a neuron, showing that networks of simple binary units could compute any logical function.
1950
Alan Turing — "Computing Machinery and Intelligence"
Turing proposed the famous "Turing Test" and asked the defining question: "Can machines think?" This paper is the philosophical cornerstone of AI.
1956
The Dartmouth Conference — AI is Born
John McCarthy, Marvin Minsky, Claude Shannon, and others met at Dartmouth College and coined the term "Artificial Intelligence." The field officially began.
1957
Frank Rosenblatt — The Perceptron
The perceptron, the first trainable neural network, was introduced. It could learn to classify simple patterns — a revolutionary concept at the time.
1969–1974
First AI Winter
Minsky & Papert's "Perceptrons" book showed limitations of single-layer networks. Funding dried up; expectations had exceeded reality.
1986
Rumelhart, Hinton & Williams — Backpropagation
The backpropagation algorithm was popularized, enabling training of multi-layer networks. Deep learning's eventual foundation was laid.
1997
Deep Blue Defeats Kasparov
IBM's Deep Blue defeated world chess champion Garry Kasparov — a landmark demonstration of AI capability, though through search rather than learning.
2006
Hinton's Deep Belief Networks — Deep Learning Revival
Geoffrey Hinton showed that deep networks could be trained effectively using layer-by-layer pre-training, reigniting excitement in neural networks.
2012
AlexNet — The ImageNet Revolution
AlexNet won ImageNet by a massive margin, halving the error rate. Deep learning became the dominant paradigm overnight. GPUs became essential.
2014
GANs & Attention Mechanisms
Ian Goodfellow introduced GANs. Bahdanau et al. introduced attention for seq2seq models — laying groundwork for Transformers.
2017
"Attention Is All You Need" — The Transformer
The Transformer architecture from Google Brain revolutionized NLP and eventually all of deep learning. Self-attention replaced recurrence.
2018–2020
BERT, GPT-2, GPT-3 — The LLM Era Begins
Pre-trained language models achieved superhuman performance on many NLP benchmarks. GPT-3's 175B parameters stunned the world.
2022–2024
ChatGPT, GPT-4, Gemini, Claude — AI Goes Mainstream
Instruction-tuned LLMs and RLHF brought AI to hundreds of millions of users. Multimodal models, agents, and code generation transformed the industry.

1.3 AI vs. Machine Learning vs. Deep Learning

These terms are often confused. Here is the precise relationship:

💡 Intuition

Think of it like cooking. AI is the broad goal of "creating a machine that cooks." Machine Learning is a specific technique: "let the machine learn recipes from examples rather than programming them manually." Deep Learning is a powerful sub-technique using deep neural networks — like using a master chef's brain (billions of interconnected taste neurons) rather than a simple recipe book.

  • AI: Any technique enabling machines to perform intelligent tasks (includes rule-based systems, search, logic, ML).
  • Machine Learning: AI systems that learn from data rather than explicit rules. The algorithm improves its performance as it sees more examples.
  • Deep Learning: ML using deep neural networks (many layers). Excels at unstructured data: images, text, audio.

2.1 Linear Algebra

Linear algebra is the foundation of virtually every ML algorithm. It gives us the tools to represent and transform data efficiently.

2.1.1 Scalars, Vectors, Matrices, Tensors

ObjectDefinitionNotationExample

2.1.2 Matrix Operations

Matrix Multiplication: The core operation of neural networks. For matrices A (m×k) and B (k×n), the product C = AB has shape (m×n):

C[i,j] = Σₖ A[i,k] · B[k,j]
🎯 Analogy

Matrix multiplication is like a "transformation machine." Every layer in a neural network applies a matrix multiplication — transforming the input space into a new representation. The matrix encodes what the layer has learned about how to transform features.

Key Matrix Properties:

  • Transpose: Aᵀ[i,j] = A[j,i] — flip rows and columns
  • Inverse: AA⁻¹ = I — only exists for square, non-singular matrices
  • Determinant: Scalar measure of how much the matrix scales area/volume
  • Rank: Number of linearly independent rows/columns
  • Trace: Sum of diagonal elements; sum of eigenvalues

2.1.3 Eigenvalues and Eigenvectors

📖 Definition

For a square matrix A, a non-zero vector v is an eigenvector if multiplying it by A only scales it (doesn't change direction):

A·v = λ·v

where λ is the corresponding eigenvalue (the scaling factor). Used in PCA, spectral clustering, and understanding network behavior.

2.1.4 Norms

Norms measure the "size" or "length" of a vector. Critical for regularization and optimization:

NormFormulaIntuitionUse in ML

L2 (Euclidean)||x||₂ = √(Σxᵢ²)Straight-line distanceRidge regularization, gradient norms
L∞ (Max)||x||∞ = max(|xᵢ|)Largest componentAdversarial attacks, clipping
Frobenius||A||_F = √(Σᵢⱼ Aᵢⱼ²)Matrix "size"Weight decay, matrix norms

2.1.5 Dot Product and Cosine Similarity

a · b = Σᵢ aᵢbᵢ = ||a|| · ||b|| · cos(θ)

The dot product measures alignment between vectors. Cosine similarity normalizes it to [-1, 1], measuring angle regardless of magnitude. Used extensively in NLP (comparing word/sentence embeddings) and recommendation systems.

2.1.6 Singular Value Decomposition (SVD)

Any matrix A (m×n) can be decomposed as:

A = U · Σ · Vᵀ

where U (m×m) and V (n×n) are orthogonal matrices, and Σ (m×n) is diagonal with non-negative singular values. SVD is foundational for PCA, recommender systems, and understanding neural network training dynamics.

2.2 Calculus & Optimization

2.2.1 Derivatives

A derivative measures the instantaneous rate of change of a function. In ML, derivatives tell us "which direction to move model parameters to reduce error."

f'(x) = lim(h→0) [f(x+h) - f(x)] / h
🎯 Analogy

Imagine you're blindfolded on a hilly landscape and want to reach the valley (minimum error). The gradient (derivative) tells you the slope under your feet — you step in the downhill direction. This is gradient descent.

2.2.2 Partial Derivatives & Gradients

When a function has multiple inputs (like a neural network with millions of parameters), we compute the partial derivative with respect to each parameter:

∇f = [∂f/∂x₁, ∂f/∂x₂, ..., ∂f/∂xₙ]

The gradient vector points in the direction of steepest ascent. We move in the negative gradient direction to minimize loss.

2.2.3 The Chain Rule

The chain rule allows us to compute derivatives of composite functions — the mathematical foundation of backpropagation:

d/dx[f(g(x))] = f'(g(x)) · g'(x)

In neural networks: if output O = f(g(h(x))), then dO/dx = (dO/df)(df/dg)(dg/dh)(dh/dx) — multiply local derivatives through the chain.

2.2.4 Gradient Descent

The update rule for parameters θ to minimize loss L:

θ ← θ - α · ∇_θ L(θ)

where α (alpha) is the learning rate — a crucial hyperparameter controlling step size.

VariantData per UpdateProsCons
Batch GDEntire datasetStable, guaranteed descentSlow for large datasets
Stochastic GD (SGD)1 sampleFast, can escape local minimaNoisy, unstable
Mini-batch GD32–512 samplesBest of both worldsNeeds tuning of batch size

2.2.5 Convexity & Local Minima

A function is convex if any line between two points lies above the function. Convex loss functions (like MSE for linear regression) have a single global minimum — easy to optimize. Most deep learning losses are non-convex with many local minima and saddle points.

2.3.4 Expectation, Variance, Covariance

E[X] = Σₓ x·P(X=x) (discrete) | ∫ x·f(x)dx (continuous)
Var[X] = E[(X - E[X])²] = E[X²] - (E[X])²

2.3.5 Maximum Likelihood Estimation (MLE)

MLE finds the parameters θ that maximize the probability of the observed data:

θ_MLE = argmax_θ P(data | θ) = argmax_θ Σᵢ log P(xᵢ | θ)

We maximize log-likelihood (log-sum instead of product for numerical stability). Most ML training objectives are equivalent to MLE under specific distributional assumptions.

2.4 Information Theory

2.4.1 Entropy

Entropy H measures the average surprise (or uncertainty) in a probability distribution:

H(P) = -Σᵢ P(xᵢ) · log₂ P(xᵢ)
💡 Intuition

A fair coin has maximum entropy (1 bit) — we're maximally uncertain. A biased coin (P(heads)=0.99) has nearly zero entropy — we know what will happen. Decision trees use entropy to measure how "mixed" a set of labels is.

2.4.2 Cross-Entropy

Cross-entropy measures the difference between true distribution P and predicted distribution Q. The most common loss function in classification:

H(P,Q) = -Σᵢ P(xᵢ) · log Q(xᵢ)

For binary classification with y ∈ {0,1} and prediction ŷ:

L = -[y·log(ŷ) + (1-y)·log(1-ŷ)]

2.4.3 KL Divergence

KL divergence measures how one probability distribution differs from another. Used in VAEs, reinforcement learning, and knowledge distillation:

D_KL(P||Q) = Σᵢ P(xᵢ) · log(P(xᵢ)/Q(xᵢ))

KL divergence is not symmetric: D_KL(P||Q) ≠ D_KL(Q||P). It equals zero when P = Q.

2.4.4 Mutual Information

I(X;Y) = H(X) - H(X|Y) = H(Y) - H(Y|X)

Mutual information measures how much knowing Y reduces uncertainty about X (and vice versa). Used in feature selection, causal discovery, and representation learning.

3.5 Feature Engineering

Raw data is rarely ready for ML. Feature engineering transforms raw inputs into representations that algorithms can learn from effectively.

  • Normalization/Standardization: Scale features to similar ranges (z-score: (x-μ)/σ, or min-max: (x-min)/(max-min))
  • One-Hot Encoding: Convert categorical variables to binary vectors
  • Binning/Discretization: Convert continuous to categorical
  • Polynomial Features: Add x², x³, x₁x₂ for non-linear relationships
  • Log Transform: Compress skewed distributions
  • Interaction Features: Products/ratios of existing features
  • Imputation: Fill missing values (mean, median, KNN, model-based)

Supervised learning is the most widely used branch of ML. Every algorithm here learns a mapping from inputs X to outputs y, guided by labeled training examples.

4.1 Linear Regression Beginner

📖 Definition

Linear Regression models the relationship between input features X and a continuous target y as a weighted sum:

ŷ = w₀ + w₁x₁ + w₂x₂ + ... + wₙxₙ = wᵀx + b
🎯 Analogy

Predicting a house price using area, bedrooms, and location. Each feature has a "weight" (how much it matters). Area might have weight ₹5000/m², bedrooms might add ₹3 lakhs each. Linear regression finds these weights automatically from data.

Loss Function: Mean Squared Error

L(w) = (1/n) · Σᵢ (yᵢ - ŷᵢ)² = (1/n) · ||y - Xw||²

Analytical Solution (Normal Equation)

For linear regression, the optimal weights have a closed-form solution:

w* = (XᵀX)⁻¹ · Xᵀy

This is computed directly — no iterative training needed! However, inverting XᵀX costs O(n³) and fails if it's singular. For large datasets, gradient descent is preferred.

Gradient Descent Solution

∂L/∂w = -(2/n) · Xᵀ(y - Xw)
w ← w - α · (∂L/∂w)

Regularization

Prevent overfitting by adding a penalty on large weights:

MethodLoss FunctionEffect
Ridge (L2)MSE + λ·||w||²Shrinks all weights; never exactly zero
Lasso (L1)MSE + λ·||w||₁Sparse weights; some become exactly zero (feature selection)
ElasticNetMSE + λ₁||w||₁ + λ₂||w||²Combines L1 and L2 benefits
Python Linear Regression from scratch + sklearn 
import numpy as np
from sklearn.linear_model import LinearRegression, Ridge
from sklearn.model_selection import train_test_split
from sklearn.metrics import mean_squared_error, r2_score

# ── From Scratch ──────────────────────────────────
class LinearRegressionScratch:
    def __init__(self, lr=0.01, n_iter=1000):
        self.lr = lr
        self.n_iter = n_iter
        self.w = None
        self.b = 0

    def fit(self, X, y):
        n, d = X.shape
        self.w = np.zeros(d)
        for _ in range(self.n_iter):
            y_pred = X @ self.w + self.b  # matrix multiply
            dw = (-2/n) * X.T @ (y - y_pred)
            db = (-2/n) * np.sum(y - y_pred)
            self.w -= self.lr * dw
            self.b -= self.lr * db

    def predict(self, X):
        return X @ self.w + self.b

# ── Sklearn ────────────────────────────────────────
X_train, X_test, y_train, y_test = train_test_split(X, y, test_size=0.2)
model = Ridge(alpha=1.0)  # Ridge = L2 regularization
model.fit(X_train, y_train)
y_pred = model.predict(X_test)
print(f"R² = {r2_score(y_test, y_pred):.4f}")
print(f"RMSE = {mean_squared_error(y_test, y_pred, squared=False):.4f}")

4.2 Logistic Regression Beginner

Despite the name, logistic regression is a classification algorithm. It predicts the probability of a class label.

🎯 Analogy

Imagine a door that swings from "definitely not spam" (0) to "definitely spam" (1). Linear regression could give you any value — even 5 or -3. Logistic regression squashes the output through a sigmoid function, constraining it to (0,1) — a valid probability.

The Sigmoid Function

σ(z) = 1 / (1 + e⁻ᶻ) where z = wᵀx + b

The sigmoid maps any real number to (0,1). Output >0.5 → class 1, <0.5 → class 0.

Loss Function: Binary Cross-Entropy

L(w) = -(1/n) · Σᵢ [yᵢ·log(ŷᵢ) + (1-yᵢ)·log(1-ŷᵢ)]

Multi-class: Softmax Regression

For K classes, softmax outputs a probability distribution:

P(y=k|x) = exp(wₖᵀx) / Σⱼ exp(wⱼᵀx)

Combined with categorical cross-entropy loss, this is used in the output layer of all multi-class neural networks.

Decision Boundary

The decision boundary is where P(y=1|x) = 0.5, which means z = wᵀx + b = 0 — a linear hyperplane in feature space. For non-linear boundaries, add polynomial features or use a neural network.

🎯 Analogy

A medical diagnosis flowchart: "Does the patient have a fever? Yes → Is the fever above 39°C? Yes → Does the patient have a rash? Yes → Likely measles." Decision trees are exactly this: a series of if-then-else questions learned from data.

A fully grown tree memorizes training data. Prevent overfitting with:

  • Post-pruning (cost-complexity): Grow full tree, then remove branches that don't improve generalization

4.4 Random Forests Intermediate

📖 Definition

Random Forests are an ensemble method that builds many decision trees on random subsets of data and features, then aggregates their predictions.

🎯 Analogy

Instead of asking one expert (one decision tree), ask a panel of 500 slightly different experts (a forest of trees). Each expert sees a random sample of the data and uses a random subset of features. Their collective majority vote is much more reliable than any single expert — this is the wisdom of the crowd.

Key Concepts

  • Bootstrap Aggregating (Bagging): Each tree trains on a bootstrap sample (n samples with replacement from training set)
  • Feature Randomness: At each split, only a random subset of √d features are considered
  • Out-of-Bag (OOB) Score: Each tree is validated on samples not in its bootstrap — free validation set
  • Prediction: Classification → majority vote; Regression → average

Why Random Forests Work: The Bias-Variance Decomposition

Error = Bias² + Variance + Irreducible Noise

Individual deep trees have low bias but high variance. Averaging many uncorrelated trees reduces variance without increasing bias significantly. The feature randomness ensures trees are decorrelated — critical for variance reduction.

Feature Importance in Random Forests

RF provides a natural feature importance ranking based on mean decrease in impurity (MDI) across all trees for each feature. Features causing the largest average reduction in impurity are most important.

4.5 Gradient Boosting Advanced

📖 Definition

Gradient Boosting builds trees sequentially, where each new tree corrects the errors of the previous ensemble. It literally "boosts" weak learners into a strong one.

🎯 Analogy

Imagine a team project. Person 1 submits a report. Person 2 reads it and writes a "correction document" fixing only the errors. Person 3 reads both and corrects remaining mistakes. Over many rounds, the collective report becomes excellent. Gradient boosting does this with decision trees — each tree corrects the residual errors of the previous ensemble.

The Algorithm

ALGORITHM: Gradient Boosting Friedman, 1999
1Initialize: F₀(x) = argmin_γ Σᵢ L(yᵢ, γ) (constant prediction)
2for m = 1 to M:
3Compute pseudo-residuals: rᵢₘ = -[∂L(yᵢ, F(xᵢ))/∂F(xᵢ)]
4Fit tree hₘ to pseudo-residuals (targets)
5Find optimal step: γₘ = argmin_γ Σᵢ L(yᵢ, Fₘ₋₁(xᵢ) + γ·hₘ(xᵢ))
6Update: Fₘ(x) = Fₘ₋₁(x) + η·γₘ·hₘ(x)
7Return Fₘ(x)

The "pseudo-residuals" are the negative gradient of the loss — exactly what we need to move the prediction toward lower loss. For MSE loss, rᵢ = yᵢ - F(xᵢ) — the actual residuals!

4.6 Support Vector Machines (SVM) Intermediate

📖 Definition

SVM finds the hyperplane that separates classes with the maximum margin — the widest possible "street" between classes.

🎯 Analogy

Imagine two groups of red and blue dots on a table. You want to draw a line separating them. Many lines would work, but SVM finds the line that gives the maximum "safety zone" (margin) on both sides. The dots closest to the boundary — the ones making the decision hardest — are called support vectors.

The Optimization Problem

Minimize: (1/2)||w||²
Subject to: yᵢ(wᵀxᵢ + b) ≥ 1 for all i

The margin width is 2/||w||. Maximizing margin = minimizing ||w||². The constraint ensures all points are classified correctly with margin ≥ 1.

Soft Margin SVM (for non-separable data)

Minimize: (1/2)||w||² + C · Σᵢ ξᵢ
Subject to: yᵢ(wᵀxᵢ + b) ≥ 1 - ξᵢ, ξᵢ ≥ 0

Slack variables ξᵢ allow violations. C controls the trade-off: high C = strict (small margin, no errors); low C = tolerant (wide margin, some errors allowed).

The Kernel Trick

SVMs can find non-linear boundaries by implicitly mapping data to high-dimensional spaces via kernel functions, without actually computing the transformation:

KernelFormulaUse Case
LinearK(x,z) = xᵀzLinearly separable, text classification
PolynomialK(x,z) = (xᵀz + c)ᵈPolynomial decision boundaries
RBF (Gaussian)K(x,z) = exp(-γ||x-z||²)Most common; smooth, non-linear
SigmoidK(x,z) = tanh(αxᵀz + c)Similar to neural network

4.7 K-Nearest Neighbors (KNN) Beginner

🎯 Analogy

You move to a new neighborhood and wonder: "Is this a good area?" You look at your K closest neighbors. If 7 out of 10 neighbors are professionals with stable incomes, you conclude it's a good area. KNN classifies a new point based on the majority class of its K nearest training points.

Algorithm

  1. Store all training data (no explicit training phase)
  2. For a new point xₙₑw, compute distance to all training points
  3. Find the K nearest neighbors
  4. Classification: majority vote; Regression: average of K neighbors

Distance Metrics

  • Euclidean: d(x,z) = √Σᵢ(xᵢ-zᵢ)² — standard straight-line distance
  • Manhattan: d(x,z) = Σᵢ|xᵢ-zᵢ| — grid-based (L1 distance)
  • Minkowski: d(x,z) = (Σᵢ|xᵢ-zᵢ|ᵖ)^(1/p) — generalization (p=1: Manhattan, p=2: Euclidean)
  • Cosine: 1 - (x·z)/(||x||·||z||) — angle-based; good for text

Choosing K

  • Small K (K=1): low bias, high variance — overfit to training data
  • Large K: high bias, low variance — smooth decision boundary
  • Use cross-validation to find optimal K; odd K avoids ties for binary classification
⚠️ Important

The Curse of Dimensionality: In high dimensions, all points become roughly equidistant from each other. KNN degrades severely with many features. Always normalize features and consider dimensionality reduction before using KNN.

4.8 Naive Bayes Beginner

Naive Bayes applies Bayes' theorem with the "naive" assumption that features are conditionally independent given the class label.

P(y|x₁,...,xₙ) ∝ P(y) · ΠᵢP(xᵢ|y)
🎯 Analogy

Spam filter: "The email contains 'FREE' and 'CLICK NOW' and 'PRIZE'." Naive Bayes computes P(spam|contains_FREE) × P(spam|contains_CLICK_NOW) × P(spam|contains_PRIZE) — treating each word independently (naive assumption). Despite this unrealistic assumption, it works remarkably well in practice for text.

CHAPTER 05

Unsupervised Learning

Unsupervised learning finds hidden structure in data without labels. It's the closest ML gets to true discovery — patterns emerging from raw data alone.

5.1 K-Means Clustering Beginner

🎯 Analogy

Organizing a bookshelf: place K markers on the shelf, assign each book to its nearest marker, then move each marker to the center of its assigned books. Repeat until the markers stop moving. K-Means does exactly this in feature space.

ALGORITHM: K-Means (Lloyd's Algorithm)
1Initialize: randomly select K points as centroids μ₁,...,μₖ
2repeat until convergence:
3Assignment: cᵢ ← argmin_k ||xᵢ - μₖ||² for each point
4Update: μₖ ← (1/|Cₖ|) Σᵢ∈Cₖ xᵢ new centroid = mean of cluster
5return cluster assignments and centroids

Choosing K: The Elbow Method

Plot Within-Cluster Sum of Squares (WCSS) vs K. The "elbow" — where the curve bends sharply — suggests the optimal K. Also use the Silhouette Score: measures cohesion vs separation (range -1 to 1, higher is better).

Limitations

  • Assumes spherical, equal-sized clusters (bad for elongated or irregular shapes)
  • Sensitive to outliers (use K-medoids instead)
  • Must specify K in advance
  • Non-deterministic (depends on initialization; use K-Means++ initialization)

5.2 DBSCAN Intermediate

📖 Definition

DBSCAN (Density-Based Spatial Clustering of Applications with Noise) defines clusters as dense regions of points separated by sparse regions. It can find arbitrarily shaped clusters and automatically detects noise/outliers.

Key Concepts

  • ε (epsilon): Neighborhood radius — how far to look for neighbors
  • MinPts: Minimum points to form a dense region
  • Core point: Has ≥ MinPts points within radius ε
  • Border point: Within ε of a core point, but has fewer neighbors
  • Noise point: Not reachable from any core point → outlier
✅ DBSCAN Advantages
  • No need to specify K
  • Finds arbitrary shapes
  • Robust to outliers (labels them as noise)
  • Works on non-convex clusters
❌ DBSCAN Limitations
  • Struggles with varying density clusters
  • Sensitive to ε and MinPts
  • Struggles in high dimensions
  • Slower than K-Means for large data

5.3 Hierarchical Clustering Intermediate

Hierarchical clustering builds a dendrogram (tree of clusters) that represents all possible clusterings at different scales. No need to pre-specify K.

ApproachProcessNotes
Agglomerative (bottom-up)Start with each point as own cluster; merge closest pairsMost common; O(n² log n)
Divisive (top-down)Start with one cluster; recursively splitLess common; computationally expensive

Linkage Methods

  • Single linkage: Distance between closest pair — tends to produce chain-like clusters
  • Complete linkage: Distance between farthest pair — compact, spherical clusters
  • Average linkage: Average distance between all pairs — compromise
  • Ward linkage: Minimize increase in total within-cluster variance — usually best

5.4 Principal Component Analysis (PCA) Intermediate

📖 Definition

PCA finds the orthogonal directions (principal components) in data that capture maximum variance, allowing dimensionality reduction with minimal information loss.

🎯 Analogy

Imagine a 3D cloud of points shaped like a pancake (flat). Most variation is in the x-y plane; very little in the z direction. PCA finds the 2 axes that best describe the pancake shape (the "principal components") and discards the z axis. You've reduced from 3D to 2D with minimal loss of information.

PCA Algorithm

  1. Standardize data: subtract mean, divide by standard deviation
  2. Compute covariance matrix: C = (1/n)XᵀX
  3. Eigen-decompose: Cv = λv (find eigenvalues λ and eigenvectors v)
  4. Sort eigenvectors by eigenvalue (descending)
  5. Project data onto top K eigenvectors

Explained Variance Ratio

Explained Variance Ratio_k = λₖ / Σᵢ λᵢ

Choose K so that cumulative explained variance ≥ 95% (or your threshold). The "scree plot" shows eigenvalues — look for an elbow.

The Bellman Equation

The fundamental recursive relationship in RL:

V(s) = max_a [R(s,a) + γ · Σₛ' P(s'|s,a) · V(s')]
Q(s,a) = R(s,a) + γ · Σₛ' P(s'|s,a) · max_a' Q(s',a')

where γ ∈ [0,1] is the discount factor — how much future rewards are valued. γ=0: short-sighted; γ=1: far-sighted.

6.2 Q-Learning

ALGORITHM: Q-Learning (Off-policy TD Control)
1Initialize Q(s,a) arbitrarily (e.g., all zeros)
2for each episode:
3Initialize state s
4for each step:
5Choose a from s using ε-greedy policy
6Take action a, observe r, s'
7Q(s,a) ← Q(s,a) + α[r + γ·max_a' Q(s',a') - Q(s,a)]
8s ← s'

6.3 Deep Q-Network (DQN)

When the state space is too large for a Q-table (e.g., Atari games with pixel inputs), use a neural network to approximate Q(s,a;θ):

  • Experience Replay: Store transitions in buffer; sample random mini-batches to break temporal correlations
  • Target Network: Use a slowly updated copy of Q-network for stable targets
  • ε-greedy Exploration: With probability ε, take random action; otherwise argmax Q

6.4 Policy Gradient Methods

Instead of learning Q-values, directly optimize the policy π(a|s;θ) using gradient ascent on expected return:

∇_θ J(θ) = E_π [∇_θ log π(aₜ|sₜ;θ) · Gₜ]

where Gₜ is the return from time t. Key algorithms: REINFORCE, PPO (Proximal Policy Optimization), A3C, SAC (Soft Actor-Critic).

Neural Networks from Scratch

Before frameworks, there was mathematics. This chapter builds a complete neural network from first principles — every equation, every gradient, every intuition.

8.1 The Perceptron

🎯 Analogy

A biological neuron receives signals from many dendrites, sums them up, and fires if the total exceeds a threshold. The perceptron is exactly this: it takes weighted inputs, adds a bias, and applies a step function. One perceptron, one decision boundary — a line in 2D.

output = step(Σᵢ wᵢxᵢ + b) = step(wᵀx + b)

8.2 Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP)

Stack multiple layers of perceptrons (with differentiable activation functions instead of step functions) to learn complex, non-linear functions.

Input Layer    Hidden Layer 1   Hidden Layer 2   Output Layer
                                
  x₁ ──┐       ●──────────●     ●──────────●
  x₂ ──┼──▶   ●──────────●  ──▶●──────────●  ──▶  ŷ
  x₃ ──┘       ●──────────●     ●──────────●
  
  3 inputs      4 neurons        4 neurons        1 output

Forward Propagation

For each layer l = 1, ..., L:

z⁽ˡ⁾ = W⁽ˡ⁾ · a⁽ˡ⁻¹⁾ + b⁽ˡ⁾ (linear transformation)
a⁽ˡ⁾ = g(z⁽ˡ⁾) (apply activation function)

The output of the network is a⁽ᴸ⁾. This is just function composition: f(x) = g_L(W_L · g_{L-1}(...g_1(W_1·x + b_1)...) + b_L).

8.3 Backpropagation

📖 Definition

Backpropagation is an efficient algorithm for computing the gradient of the loss with respect to every weight in the network, using the chain rule applied backward through the computation graph.

💡 Intuition

Backpropagation answers: "If I slightly increase weight w_{ij}, how much does the loss change?" It computes this for every single weight — millions or billions of them — in time proportional to a forward pass. Without it, training deep networks would be intractable.

The Backprop Equations

Define δ⁽ˡ⁾ = ∂L/∂z⁽ˡ⁾ (the "error signal" at layer l):

δ⁽ᴸ⁾ = ∂L/∂a⁽ᴸ⁾ ⊙ g'(z⁽ᴸ⁾) (output layer)
δ⁽ˡ⁾ = (W⁽ˡ⁺¹⁾)ᵀ δ⁽ˡ⁺¹⁾ ⊙ g'(z⁽ˡ⁾) (hidden layers)
∂L/∂W⁽ˡ⁾ = δ⁽ˡ⁾ · (a⁽ˡ⁻¹⁾)ᵀ
∂L/∂b⁽ˡ⁾ = δ⁽ˡ⁾

where ⊙ denotes element-wise multiplication. These four equations completely describe backpropagation.

CHAPTER 09

Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs)

CNNs exploit the spatial structure of images through weight sharing and local connectivity — allowing them to detect features regardless of where they appear.

9.1 The Convolution Operation

🎯 Analogy

A convolution is like sliding a magnifying glass (the filter/kernel) over an image. The filter looks for a specific pattern — maybe an edge, a corner, a texture. At each position, it computes how strongly that pattern is present. Different filters detect different patterns.

(I * K)[i,j] = Σₘ Σₙ I[i+m, j+n] · K[m,n]

Key Concepts

  • Filter/Kernel: Small matrix of learnable weights (e.g., 3×3, 5×5) that detects a specific feature
  • Feature Map: Output of applying a filter to the input
  • Padding: Add zeros around the input border to control output size. "Same" padding preserves spatial size.
  • Stride: Step size when sliding the filter. Stride=2 halves the spatial dimensions.
  • Channels: Input might have 3 (RGB); output has C_out channels (one per filter)

Output Size Formula

output_size = ⌊(input_size + 2·padding - kernel_size) / stride⌋ + 1

Recurrent Networks, LSTMs & GRUs

Sequential data — text, time series, audio — has order that matters. Recurrent networks process sequences step-by-step, maintaining a "memory" of past inputs.

10.1 Vanilla RNN

🎯 Analogy

Reading a book word by word. Each new word updates your understanding of the current sentence. Your "mental state" carries the context forward. An RNN maintains a hidden state hₜ that summarizes everything seen so far.

hₜ = tanh(Wₕhₜ₋₁ + Wₓxₜ + b)
yₜ = Wᵧhₜ

The Vanishing Gradient Problem

When backpropagating through time over many steps, gradients are multiplied by Wₕ at each step. If ||Wₕ|| < 1, gradients shrink exponentially → vanishing gradients (can't learn long-range dependencies). If ||Wₕ|| > 1, gradients explode.

10.2 Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM)

📖 Definition

LSTM (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997) solves the vanishing gradient problem with a special cell state — a "conveyor belt" carrying information across time with multiplicative gates controlling what to remember and forget.

LSTM Gate Equations

fₜ = σ(Wf · [hₜ₋₁, xₜ] + bf) [Forget Gate: what to discard from cell]
iₜ = σ(Wi · [hₜ₋₁, xₜ] + bi) [Input Gate: what new info to write]
C̃ₜ = tanh(WC · [hₜ₋₁, xₜ] + bC) [Candidate cell state]
Cₜ = fₜ ⊙ Cₜ₋₁ + iₜ ⊙ C̃ₜ [Cell state update]
oₜ = σ(Wo · [hₜ₋₁, xₜ] + bo) [Output Gate: what to expose]
hₜ = oₜ ⊙ tanh(Cₜ) [Hidden state]

The cell state Cₜ flows with only element-wise operations (⊙ = Hadamard product) — allowing gradients to flow without vanishing. σ gates learn when to let information through.

10.3 Gated Recurrent Unit (GRU)

GRU (Cho et al., 2014) simplifies LSTM by merging the forget and input gates into a single update gate, and merging cell and hidden state. Fewer parameters, similar performance:

zₜ = σ(Wz · [hₜ₋₁, xₜ]) [Update gate]
rₜ = σ(Wr · [hₜ₋₁, xₜ]) [Reset gate]
h̃ₜ = tanh(W · [rₜ⊙hₜ₋₁, xₜ]) [Candidate hidden]
hₜ = (1-zₜ)⊙hₜ₋₁ + zₜ⊙h̃ₜ [Final hidden state]

10.4 Sequence-to-Sequence (Seq2Seq)

For tasks like machine translation, we need to map a variable-length input sequence to a variable-length output sequence (different lengths!):

Input:  "Je  suis  étudiant"
         ↓     ↓       ↓
      [Encoder RNN]  →  context vector c
                               ↓
Output: "I"    "am"  "student"
         ↑      ↑       ↑
       [Decoder RNN (conditioned on c)]

The bottleneck: the entire input sequence must be compressed into a single fixed-size context vector c. Attention was invented to solve this limitation.

CHAPTER 11

Attention & Transformers

The Transformer architecture, introduced in 2017, has become the foundation of modern AI. Understanding attention from first principles is essential for understanding GPT, BERT, and every modern LLM.

11.1 The Attention Mechanism

🎯 Analogy

When you read "The cat sat on the mat because it was tired," you know "it" refers to "cat" not "mat" — your attention naturally connects "it" back to "cat." Attention mechanisms allow neural networks to do the same: when processing each word, they look back at all other words and decide which are most relevant.

Bahdanau Attention (2015)

For each decoder step, compute a weighted sum of all encoder states:

eᵢⱼ = a(sᵢ₋₁, hⱼ) [alignment score: how relevant is encoder state j?]
αᵢⱼ = softmax(eᵢⱼ) [normalize to probabilities]
cᵢ = Σⱼ αᵢⱼ · hⱼ [context vector: weighted sum of encoder states]

Scaled Dot-Product Attention

The Transformer introduces a cleaner formulation using Query, Key, Value (Q, K, V):

💡 Intuition — The Library Analogy

Query (Q): What you're looking for (your search term).
Key (K): The index cards for each document (what each position "advertises").
Value (V): The actual content of each document.
Attention computes: how well does each Key match my Query? → weight the Values by that match.

Attention(Q, K, V) = softmax(QKᵀ / √dₖ) · V

The scaling factor √dₖ (where dₖ is the key dimension) prevents the dot products from getting too large, which would push softmax into regions with tiny gradients.

Multi-Head Attention

Run attention h times in parallel with different learned projections, then concatenate:

MultiHead(Q,K,V) = Concat(head₁,...,headₕ) · Wᴼ
headᵢ = Attention(Q·Wᵢᵠ, K·Wᵢᴷ, V·Wᵢᵛ)

Different heads can attend to different aspects: one might track syntactic relationships, another semantic similarity, another coreference. This is the power of multi-head attention.

Positional Encoding

Self-attention is permutation invariant — it doesn't inherently know position. Positional encodings inject position information:

PE(pos, 2i) = sin(pos / 10000^(2i/dmodel))
PE(pos, 2i+1) = cos(pos / 10000^(2i/dmodel))

Modern models (GPT-4, LLaMA) use Rotary Position Embeddings (RoPE) or ALiBi for better length generalization.

Feed-Forward Network in Transformer

FFN(x) = max(0, xW₁ + b₁)W₂ + b₂

The FFN has inner dimension dff = 4×dmodel. It processes each position independently — this is where much of the "knowledge" is stored (the attention gathers context, the FFN processes it).

Layer Normalization & Residual Connections

Every sub-layer has: output = LayerNorm(x + Sublayer(x)). The residual helps gradient flow; LayerNorm stabilizes training. Modern models use Pre-LayerNorm (norm before attention/FFN) for better training stability.

Advanced Deep Learning Techniques

Beyond the basics — transfer learning, knowledge distillation, neural architecture search, and the tricks that make deep learning work at scale.

12.1 Transfer Learning

🎯 Analogy

A doctor who studied general medicine for 10 years can specialize in cardiology much faster than someone who starts from scratch. Transfer learning reuses knowledge from a pre-trained model (trained on massive data) as a starting point for a new, specific task.

Strategies

  • Feature extraction: Freeze all pre-trained weights; only train a new classification head. Use when data is small and similar to pretraining domain.
  • Fine-tuning: Start from pre-trained weights; continue training all layers (or last few) on new task. Most common approach.
  • Domain adaptation: When source and target domains differ significantly — use techniques like DANN or adversarial training to align representations.

12.2 Knowledge Distillation

Train a smaller "student" model to mimic the outputs of a larger "teacher" model. The student learns from soft targets (probability distributions) rather than hard labels, capturing the teacher's knowledge about class relationships.

L_distill = α · L_CE(y, y_student) + (1-α) · T² · KL(y_teacher/T, y_student/T)

Temperature T > 1 softens the probability distributions, revealing more information about class similarities. Used to create efficient mobile models (DistilBERT, etc.).

12.3 Self-Supervised Learning

Create labels automatically from data by masking parts and predicting them. Used to pre-train large models on unlabeled data:

  • Masked Language Modeling (BERT): Mask 15% of tokens, predict the masked tokens
  • Next Sentence Prediction (BERT): Predict if two sentences are adjacent
  • Autoregressive LM (GPT): Predict the next token given all previous tokens
  • Contrastive Learning (SimCLR, MoCo): Pull together augmented views of same image; push apart different images
  • Masked Image Modeling (MAE): Mask 75% of image patches; reconstruct them

12.4 Mixture of Experts (MoE)

Instead of using the entire network for every input, route each token to a subset of "expert" FFN layers using a learned router. Enables massive parameter count (for capacity) without proportional compute increase. Used in GPT-4, Mixtral, Switch Transformer.

y = Σₖ G(x)_k · Eₖ(x)

where G(x) is the gating network (router) selecting top-k experts, and Eₖ are the expert networks.

CHAPTER 13

Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs)

GANs pit two neural networks against each other in a game-theoretic competition — one generates fake data, the other tries to detect fakes. The result: remarkably realistic generated content.

13.1 The GAN Framework

🎯 Analogy

A counterfeiter (Generator) tries to create fake money. A detective (Discriminator) examines bills and decides if they're real or fake. As the counterfeiter improves, the detective gets better at spotting subtle flaws. This forces the counterfeiter to improve further. Eventually, the fakes become indistinguishable from real bills.

min_G max_D V(D,G) = E[log D(x)] + E[log(1 - D(G(z)))]

The Components

  • Generator G: Takes random noise z ~ p(z) → produces fake samples G(z). Goal: fool the discriminator.
  • Discriminator D: Takes real or fake sample → outputs P(real). Goal: distinguish real from fake.
  • Nash Equilibrium: Training converges when D can't do better than random guessing (D(x)=0.5 for all x) — the generator has perfectly learned the data distribution.

GAN Training Instabilities

  • Mode collapse: Generator produces limited variety — always outputs same few images
  • Training oscillation: Loss bounces without converging
  • Vanishing gradients: When D is too good, G gets no gradient signal

CHAPTER 14

Variational Autoencoders (VAEs)

VAEs learn compressed, structured latent representations of data — enabling smooth interpolation, controlled generation, and disentangled feature learning.

14.1 Autoencoders First

An autoencoder compresses input x into a latent code z (encoder) and reconstructs x from z (decoder):

  Input x → [Encoder] → z (bottleneck) → [Decoder] → x̂ ≈ x
                              ↑
                    low-dimensional latent space

14.2 The VAE Model

Instead of a fixed point z, the encoder outputs a distribution q(z|x) = N(μ(x), σ²(x)). Sample z from this distribution to generate x̂.

The ELBO (Evidence Lower Bound)

L(θ,φ;x) = E[log p_θ(x|z)] - D_KL(q_φ(z|x) || p(z))
  • Reconstruction term: E[log p(x|z)] — how well do we reconstruct x? (like autoencoder loss)
  • KL regularization: D_KL(q||p) — force the posterior to be close to the prior N(0,I)

The Reparameterization Trick

Sampling from q(z|x) is not differentiable. The reparameterization trick makes it differentiable:

z = μ(x) + ε·σ(x) where ε ~ N(0,I)

Now gradients flow through μ and σ, and the randomness is in ε (a fixed external noise). This enables backpropagation through the sampling step.

CHAPTER 15

Diffusion Models

Diffusion models are the engine behind Stable Diffusion, DALL-E 2/3, and Midjourney. They learn to reverse a noise-adding process — essentially learning to "see" images in noise.

15.1 The Core Idea

🎯 Analogy

Imagine a beautiful sand sculpture slowly eroding in the wind — gradually becoming a pile of random sand. A diffusion model learns to reverse this process: given a pile of random sand (noise), reconstruct the original sculpture (image). The model learns "what step in the erosion process does this look like, and what did it look like one step earlier?"

Forward Process (Diffusion)

Gradually add Gaussian noise to data x₀ over T timesteps:

q(xₜ|xₜ₋₁) = N(xₜ; √(1-βₜ)·xₜ₋₁, βₜ·I)

With noise schedule β₁ < β₂ < ... < βT, at T the image is pure noise N(0,I). Key: can sample xₜ directly from x₀:

xₜ = √ᾱₜ · x₀ + √(1-ᾱₜ) · ε where ε~N(0,I), ᾱₜ = Πᵢ(1-βᵢ)

Reverse Process (Denoising)

Learn a neural network ε_θ(xₜ, t) to predict the noise ε at each timestep:

L_simple = E_{t,x₀,ε} [||ε - ε_θ(xₜ, t)||²]

Remarkably simple: just predict the added noise! The network is typically a U-Net with time conditioning.

15.2 Classifier-Free Guidance (CFG)

For text-conditioned generation (DALL-E, Stable Diffusion), use CFG to improve conditioning quality:

ε_guided = ε_θ(xₜ, t) + w · (ε_θ(xₜ, t, c) - ε_θ(xₜ, t))

w is the guidance scale (typically 7-15). Higher w → images more faithful to text prompt but less diverse. This is the "CFG scale" you see in image generation interfaces.

CHAPTER 16

Large Language Models (LLMs)

LLMs are transformer models trained on vast text corpora to predict the next token. Emerging from this simple objective are reasoning, coding, translation, and conversation capabilities that approach or surpass human performance on many benchmarks.

16.1 Pretraining

The Language Modeling Objective

L = -Σᵢ log P(xᵢ | x₁, x₂, ..., xᵢ₋₁)

Predict the next token given all previous tokens. This is called autoregressive language modeling. With enough data and parameters, the model must implicitly learn grammar, facts, reasoning, and world knowledge to minimize this loss.

Scaling Laws

The Chinchilla scaling laws (Hoffmann et al., 2022) show that model performance scales predictably with compute, parameters N, and tokens D:

L(N, D) ≈ E + A/N^α + B/D^β

Key finding: for a given compute budget, train a smaller model on more tokens. The optimal ratio is roughly 20 tokens per parameter (e.g., a 7B model should train on 140B tokens).

16.2 Fine-Tuning & RLHF

Instruction Fine-Tuning (SFT)

Fine-tune the base model on (instruction, response) pairs to follow user instructions. Base models are trained to predict text; instruction-tuned models are trained to be helpful assistants.

Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF)

  1. SFT: Fine-tune on demonstration data
     Prompt → [SFT Model] → Response (human-written)

  2. Reward Modeling: Train RM from human preferences
     (Prompt, Response A, Response B) → human picks better
     → Train RM to predict preference

  3. PPO: Optimize policy with RL + KL constraint
     Prompt → [Policy] → Response → [Reward Model] → Scalar reward
     → Update policy to maximize reward - β·KL(policy||SFT)

Object Detection

Object detection simultaneously answers "what is in this image?" and "where is it?" — localizing objects with bounding boxes.

19.1 Problem Formulation

Given an image, output a list of (class, x, y, w, h, confidence) tuples for each detected object. Key metrics:

  • IoU (Intersection over Union): (Area of Overlap) / (Area of Union) — measures bounding box quality. IoU > 0.5 typically considered a correct detection.
  • mAP (mean Average Precision): Average of AP over all classes, over IoU thresholds
  • NMS (Non-Maximum Suppression): Remove duplicate detections by keeping only the highest-confidence box among overlapping boxes

19.2 Detection Paradigms

The YOLO (You Only Look Once) approach: divide the image into an S×S grid. Each grid cell predicts B bounding boxes (x,y,w,h,confidence) and C class probabilities. Everything is predicted in a single forward pass — hence "you only look once."

19.3 Feature Pyramid Networks (FPN)

Different objects have different scales. FPN builds a multi-scale feature pyramid by combining high-level semantic features (small feature maps) with low-level spatial features (large feature maps) via top-down pathways and lateral connections. All modern detectors use FPN or a variant.

📖 Definition

21.1 Vision Transformer (ViT)

The key insight: split an image into 16×16 patches, flatten each patch, project to an embedding, and feed the sequence of patch embeddings to a standard transformer encoder (with positional embeddings).

Image (224×224×3) → 196 patches of 16×16×3 → 196 × 768-dim embeddings → Transformer
💡 Intuition

ViT treats image patches like words in a sentence. Self-attention allows every patch to directly attend to every other patch — learning long-range spatial relationships that convolutions only capture through many stacked layers. Given enough data (JFT-300M), ViT surpasses CNNs.

21.3 CLIP: Bridging Vision and Language

CLIP (Contrastive Language-Image Pretraining) trains a vision encoder and text encoder simultaneously to align matched image-text pairs in a shared embedding space:

L = -Σᵢ [log sim(v_i, t_i) / Σⱼ sim(v_i, t_j) + log sim(v_i, t_i) / Σⱼ sim(v_j, t_i)]

Zero-shot classification: embed class names as text, embed image, pick the class with highest cosine similarity. No training on the target task — remarkable generalization.

22.3 Classical Text Features

Bag-of-Words (BoW)

Represent a document as a vector of word counts. Loses word order but captures presence. Combined with TF-IDF for better performance.

TF-IDF

TF-IDF(t,d) = TF(t,d) × IDF(t)
TF(t,d) = count(t in d) / total words in d
IDF(t) = log(N / df(t)) where N=corpus size, df=docs containing t

Upweights rare, discriminative words; downweights common words (like "the"). The workhorse of classical IR.

N-grams

Contiguous sequences of N tokens. Bigrams ("New York") and trigrams capture some context that BoW misses. Feature dimensionality grows quickly with N.

Word Embeddings & Representation Learning

Dense vector representations that encode semantic relationships — the bridge between discrete symbols and the continuous space of neural networks.

23.1 Word2Vec

🎯 Analogy

"You shall know a word by the company it keeps" (Firth, 1957). Word2Vec learns that "king" and "queen" both appear near "royal" and "throne" — so they end up nearby in vector space. The famous result: king - man + woman ≈ queen.

ArchitectureTaskInput → Output
CBOWPredict center word from contextContext words → Center word
Skip-gramPredict context from center wordCenter word → Context words

Training Skip-gram

L = Σ_t Σ_{-c≤j≤c, j≠0} log P(wₜ₊ⱼ | wₜ)
P(wₒ | wᵢ) = exp(vwₒᵀ · vwᵢ) / Σ_w exp(vwᵀ · vwᵢ)

Negative sampling makes this efficient: treat it as binary classification (actual context vs. random "noise" words).

23.2 GloVe (Global Vectors)

GloVe factorizes the log of the word co-occurrence matrix. Combines global statistics (co-occurrence counts over entire corpus) with local context windows:

L = Σᵢⱼ f(Xᵢⱼ) · (wᵢᵀw̃ⱼ + bᵢ + b̃ⱼ - log Xᵢⱼ)²

23.3 Contextual Embeddings

Word2Vec and GloVe assign a single vector per word regardless of context. "Bank" (river) and "bank" (financial) get the same vector — a fundamental limitation. Contextual embeddings solve this.

CHAPTER 24

Sequence Models for NLP

Covering the complete evolution from classical sequence models to the encoder-decoder architectures that enabled modern MT and summarization.

24.1 Sequence-to-Sequence with Attention

Seq2seq with Bahdanau attention dramatically improved machine translation by letting the decoder attend to different encoder positions at each decoding step. This was the direct predecessor of the Transformer.

24.2 CRF (Conditional Random Fields)

For sequence labeling tasks (NER, POS tagging), CRFs model the joint probability of an entire label sequence — enforcing valid label transitions (e.g., I-PER can't follow B-LOC). Often combined with LSTM (BiLSTM-CRF) as the go-to NER architecture before transformers.

24.3 Named Entity Recognition (NER)

Tag entities in text with types (Person, Organization, Location, Date, etc.). Modern approach: fine-tune a BERT model with a token classification head (linear layer per token → BIO tagging scheme).

  Text:    Barack  Obama  was  born  in  Hawaii
  Labels:   B-PER   I-PER   O     O    O    B-LOC

CHAPTER 25

BERT, GPT, T5 & Modern NLP

The three families of pre-trained language models — encoder-only, decoder-only, and encoder-decoder — that define the modern NLP landscape.

25.1 BERT (Encoder-Only)

BERT (Bidirectional Encoder Representations from Transformers, Devlin et al. 2018) uses a Transformer encoder with two pretraining objectives:

  • Masked Language Modeling (MLM): Randomly mask 15% of tokens; predict the masked tokens. Creates deep bidirectional context.
  • Next Sentence Prediction (NSP): Predict whether two sentences are consecutive (now known to be less important)

BERT Fine-tuning Pattern

  Downstream Task Heads:

  Classification:    [CLS] → Linear(hidden, num_classes)
  NER/Tagging:       [tok1, tok2, ...] → Linear(hidden, num_labels) per token
  QA (extractive):  [CLS, question, SEP, context] → start/end span logits
  Sentence Pairs:   [CLS, sent1, SEP, sent2, SEP] → [CLS] for classification

25.2 GPT (Decoder-Only)

GPT series uses a Transformer decoder with causal (left-to-right) attention. Pretrained on next-token prediction. The scaling revolution:

  • GPT-1 (2018): 117M params — showed promise of unsupervised pretraining
  • GPT-2 (2019): 1.5B params — surprising few-shot generation quality
  • GPT-3 (2020): 175B params — demonstrated in-context learning at scale
  • InstructGPT (2022): RLHF alignment — became the ChatGPT foundation
  • GPT-4 (2023): Multimodal, ~1.8T MoE — frontier capability

25.3 T5 (Encoder-Decoder)

T5 (Text-To-Text Transfer Transformer, Raffel et al. 2020) frames ALL NLP tasks as text-to-text:

  Translation:    "translate English to German: How are you?" → "Wie geht es dir?"
  Summarization:  "summarize: [long article]" → "[short summary]"
  QA:             "question: What is the capital? context: France is..." → "Paris"
  Classification: "sentiment: This movie was amazing." → "positive"

This unified framework simplifies transfer learning — one model, one training recipe, many tasks. T5 variants (mT5, Flan-T5, UL2) remain state-of-the-art for many tasks.

27.2 Inference Optimization

  • Quantization: Reduce weight precision (FP32 → FP16/INT8/INT4). 4x memory, 2-4x speedup with <1% accuracy loss. QAT (Quantization-Aware Training) vs PTQ (Post-Training Quantization).
  • Pruning: Remove small/unimportant weights (structured: entire neurons/filters; unstructured: individual weights)
  • Knowledge Distillation: Smaller student model mimics larger teacher
  • TensorRT (NVIDIA): Compile and optimize neural networks for GPU inference; layer fusion, kernel autotuning
  • Batching: Process multiple requests together; dynamic batching maximizes throughput
  • KV Cache: Cache key-value pairs in transformer attention for autoregressive generation; avoids recomputation

from fastapi import FastAPI, HTTPException
from pydantic import BaseModel
import torch
import numpy as np
from contextlib import asynccontextmanager

# ── Startup: load model once ──────────────────────
model = None

@asynccontextmanager
async def lifespan(app: FastAPI):
    global model
    model = torch.load("model.pt", map_location="cuda")
    model.eval()
    yield

app = FastAPI(lifespan=lifespan)

class PredictRequest(BaseModel):
    features: list[float]

class PredictResponse(BaseModel):
    prediction: float
    confidence: float

@app.post("/predict", response_model=PredictResponse)
async def predict(request: PredictRequest):
    with torch.no_grad():
        x = torch.tensor(request.features).float().unsqueeze(0)
        logits = model(x)
        probs = torch.nn.functional.softmax(logits, dim=-1)
        conf, pred = probs.max(dim=-1)
    return PredictResponse(
        prediction=pred.item(),
        confidence=conf.item()
    )

Monitoring, Data Drift & Model Maintenance

Models degrade silently in production as the world changes. This chapter covers the monitoring systems that catch degradation before it causes harm.

28.1 Why Models Degrade

TypeDefinitionExample

28.2 Drift Detection Methods

  • Population Stability Index (PSI): Measure shift in feature distribution between training and production
  • KS Test (Kolmogorov-Smirnov): Statistical test for distribution equality
  • Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD): Kernel-based distribution comparison
  • CUSUM/ADWIN: Sequential tests for detecting shifts in data streams
  • Model-based: Train a classifier to distinguish training vs. production data — if it succeeds, there's drift

28.3 Observability Stack

  • Input monitoring: Track feature distributions, missing rates, schema violations
  • Prediction monitoring: Track prediction distributions, confidence scores
  • Business metrics: Track downstream outcomes (conversion rate, fraud caught, user satisfaction)
  • Latency/throughput: P50/P95/P99 response times; requests per second; error rates
  • Shadow mode: Run new model in parallel; compare outputs without affecting users
  • A/B testing: Route % of traffic to new model; measure metric improvement with statistical significance

28.4 Model Retraining Strategies

  • Scheduled retraining: Retrain on fixed schedule (weekly, monthly)
  • Triggered retraining: Retrain when drift is detected above threshold
  • Continuous training: Ongoing training on streaming data
  • Champion-Challenger: Always run new model candidate vs. current champion
