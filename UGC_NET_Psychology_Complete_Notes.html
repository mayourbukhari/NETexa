Unit 1: Emergence of Psychology

Psychological Thought in Eastern Systems ▼

Eastern philosophy has contributed significantly to psychological understanding through various ancient systems:

Bhagavad Gita Hindu philosophical text emphasizing mental discipline, duty (Dharma), and self-realization. Key concepts include:
  • Yoga of action (Karma Yoga) - dispassionate action
  • Yoga of knowledge (Jnana Yoga) - understanding self
  • Yoga of devotion (Bhakti Yoga) - emotional discipline
  • Mind control and emotional regulation through practice
Buddhism Offers a systematic approach to understanding consciousness and suffering:
  • Four Noble Truths - nature of suffering
  • Eight-fold path - practical psychology of ethical living
  • Mindfulness (Sati) and meditation for mental development
  • Concept of non-self (Anatman) challenging ego psychology
  • Five aggregates (Skandhas) - components of human experience
Sufism Islamic mystical tradition focusing on:
  • Spiritual psychology and transformation
  • Ego dissolution and self-transcendence
  • Heart-centered knowledge
  • Stages of spiritual development
  • Integration of emotion and intellect
Integral Yoga (Sri Aurobindo) Synthesis of spirituality and modern psychology:
  • Evolution of consciousness
  • Integration of physical, mental, and spiritual aspects
  • Transcendence through integral development
  • Supramental consciousness as goal

Western Psychology: Historical Development ▼

Greek Heritage
  • Plato (428-348 BCE) - Theory of forms; immortal soul; tripartite soul (reason, spirit, appetite)
  • Aristotle (384-322 BCE) - Empirical observation; soul as form of body; actuality and potentiality
  • Hippocrates (460-370 BCE) - Humoral theory; biological basis of temperament
Medieval Period
  • Scholasticism - Integration of Aristotelian logic with theology
  • Islamic scholars - Preservation and advancement of Greek knowledge
  • St. Thomas Aquinas - Soul and body relationship
Modern Period Foundations
  • Descartes (1596-1650) - Mind-body dualism; cogito ergo sum
  • Locke (1632-1704) - Empiricism; tabula rasa (blank slate)
  • Hume (1711-1776) - Association of ideas
  • Kant (1724-1804) - Categories of mind; synthetic knowledge
Major Schools of Western Psychology
School Focus Key Figures Core Concepts
Structuralism Structure of consciousness Wundt, Titchener Introspection; elements of consciousness; sensations, images, feelings
Functionalism Purpose of consciousness James, Dewey Adaptive behavior; stream of consciousness; mental operations

Four Founding Paths of Academic Psychology
  • Wilhelm Wundt (1832-1920): Founder of experimental psychology; established first psychology laboratory (1879)
  • Sigmund Freud (1856-1939): Psychoanalysis; unconscious mind; psychosexual development
  • William James (1842-1910): American psychology; functionalism; stream of consciousness
  • Wilhelm Dilthey (1833-1911): Hermeneutics; understanding vs. explanation; idiographic approach
Crisis in Psychology The strict adherence to experimental-analytical paradigm (logical empiricism) led to:
  • Reductionism - oversimplification of complex phenomena
  • Inability to address meaningful human experiences
  • Neglect of subjective meaning and consciousness
  • Limited applicability to real-world problems
  • Rise of alternative paradigms (qualitative, phenomenological)
Remember This Contrast between Wundt's laboratory psychology and Wundt's Völkerpsychologie (folk psychology) - he believed some psychological phenomena could only be understood historically and culturally.

  • What counts as valid knowledge?
  • Role of observer in knowledge production

  • How is knowledge validated?

  • Experimental vs. Natural observation

  • Role of statistics and interpretation

💡 Key Questions for Revision

Q1: Discuss the evolution of academic psychology in India from pre-independence to 2000s.

Answer:

Pre-Independence (until 1947): Psychology was primarily philosophical and spiritual in nature, taught as part of philosophy departments. Little scientific rigor; traditional Indian systems dominated.

Post-Independence (1947-1970): Formal psychology departments established; heavy influence from Western psychology; adoption of experimental methods; focus on universal principles; neglect of indigenous systems.

1970s - Social Issues Focus: Recognition of psychology's role in addressing social problems; community psychology emerged; attention to rural psychology; development of applied areas.

1980s - Indigenization Movement: Conscious effort to integrate Indian philosophical and spiritual systems with academic psychology; recognition of Emic perspective; Indian concepts studied scientifically.

1990s - Identity Crisis: Questioning disciplinary boundaries; recognition of multiple paradigms; efforts to establish distinct Indian psychology identity; interdisciplinary approaches.

2000s - Emergence of Indian Psychology: Recognition as legitimate sub-discipline; research on concepts like Yoga, Bhagavad Gita, Buddhist psychology; international recognition.

Q2: Compare Western and Indian approaches to psychological knowledge.

Answer:

Western Approach:

  • Focus on external, objective reality
  • Scientific method, experimental design
  • Emphasis on universal principles
  • Reductionist, analytical
  • Mind-body dualism (historically)
  • Secular, materialist orientation

Indian Approach:

  • Focus on subjective experience and consciousness
  • Experiential and contemplative methods
  • Recognition of individual uniqueness
  • Holistic, integrated approach
  • Mind-body-spirit unity
  • Integration with spirituality and ethics
Q3: Explain the crisis in psychology due to strict adherence to experimental-analytical paradigm.

Answer:

Psychology's over-reliance on logical empiricism and experimental methods created several problems:

Limitations:

  • Reductionism: Complex human phenomena reduced to simple variables, losing nuance
  • Laboratory Artificiality: Findings from labs often don't translate to real-world settings
  • Neglect of Meaning: Focus on measurable behavior ignores subjective meaning and experience
  • Determinism: Mechanistic view denies human agency and free will
  • Cultural Blindness: Universal principles ignore cultural context and differences
  • Limited Scope: Spiritual, existential, and transcendent experiences excluded from study

Response: Led to emergence of alternative paradigms (qualitative, phenomenological, critical) and recognition of psychology's limitations in understanding full spectrum of human experience.

Unit 2: Research Methodology and Statistics

Research Fundamentals ▼

Research Systematic investigation to discover or verify facts, establish relationships, or develop theories.
Meaning of Research
  • From "re-search" = searching again/afresh
  • Systematic, controlled, empirical investigation
  • Aims to understand, predict, or control phenomena
  • Based on observation and experimentation
Purpose of Research
  • Exploratory: Discover new phenomena; preliminary investigation
  • Descriptive: Describe characteristics of phenomena; what exists
  • Explanatory: Explain relationships and causes; why things happen
  • Predictive: Predict future behavior; what will happen
  • Applied: Solve practical problems; improve conditions
Dimensions of Research
  • Basic vs. Applied: Fundamental knowledge vs. practical solutions
  • Quantitative vs. Qualitative: Numbers vs. meanings
  • Experimental vs. Non-experimental: Manipulation vs. observation
  • Etic vs. Emic: Outsider vs. insider perspective
  • Nomothetic vs. Idiographic: General laws vs. individual cases
Key Concepts in Research
Variables Characteristics that can take different values:
  • Independent Variable (IV): Variable manipulated by researcher; cause
  • Dependent Variable (DV): Variable measured; effect
  • Moderating Variable: Affects relationship between IV and DV
  • Confounding Variable: Uncontrolled variable affecting results
  • Mediating Variable: Through which effect occurs
Operational Definitions
  • Precise definition of variable in terms of operations/procedures to measure it
  • Example: "Anxiety" operationally defined as score on State-Trait Anxiety Inventory
  • Makes variables measurable and concrete
  • Enables replication of research
Hypothesis Testable prediction about relationship between variables:
  • Null Hypothesis (H0): No relationship/difference exists
  • Alternative Hypothesis (H1): Relationship/difference exists
  • Directional: Predicts direction of relationship
  • Non-directional: Predicts relationship exists but not direction
Sampling Process of selecting subset from population:
  • Population: Entire group of interest
  • Sample: Subset of population
  • Sampling Frame: List from which sample drawn
  • Probability Sampling: Random selection; representative
  • Non-probability Sampling: Non-random; may be biased
Ethics in Research
  • Informed Consent: Participants understand research and risks
  • Confidentiality: Participant information protected
  • Beneficence: Research benefits outweigh risks
  • Justice: Fair distribution of research benefits/burdens
  • No Deception: Unless absolutely necessary and justified
  • Right to Withdraw: Participants can stop at any time
  • Institutional Review Board (IRB): Approval required before research
Remember The four principles of research ethics: Autonomy (informed consent), Beneficence (maximize benefits), Non-maleficence (minimize harm), Justice (fair treatment).

Paradigms and Methods of Research ▼

Quantitative Research
  • Numbers and statistics as primary data
  • Objective, controlled experimentation
  • Large sample sizes
  • Statistical analysis and generalization
  • Hypothesis testing
  • Strengths: Objective, generalizable, precise
  • Limitations: Loses nuance, artificial settings, reductionist
Qualitative Research
  • Words, meanings, and descriptions as primary data
  • Natural settings, participant immersion
  • Small, purposive samples
  • Thematic and interpretive analysis
  • Exploration and discovery
  • Strengths: Rich detail, contextual understanding, flexible
  • Limitations: Subjective, limited generalizability, time-consuming
Mixed Methods Approach
  • Integration of quantitative and qualitative data
  • Triangulation - corroboration from multiple sources
  • Sequential (one then other) or concurrent (simultaneous)
  • Complementary strengths of both approaches
  • Example: Survey (quantitative) + interviews (qualitative)
Major Research Methods
Method Definition Advantages Disadvantages
Observation Systematic watching and recording of behavior Natural behavior, rich data, flexible Observer bias, limited scope, time-consuming
Survey Questionnaires/interviews gathering self-report data Large samples, efficient, standardizable Social desirability bias, self-report limitations
Experiment Researcher manipulates IV, measures DV Establish causality, controlled, replicable Artificial, limited external validity, ethical concerns
Quasi-Experiment No random assignment, some control More natural, practical, causal inference Cannot rule out all alternative explanations
Field Study Research in natural real-world setting High external validity, natural behavior Less control, confounding variables, costly
Cross-Cultural Study Comparison across different cultures Identify cultural universals/differences Complex design, multiple confounds, interpretation
Phenomenology In-depth study of lived experience Deep understanding of meaning, authentic Small samples, interpretive, not generalizable
Grounded Theory Theory developed from data systematically Generate new theories, data-driven Requires skill, time-consuming, emerging validity
Focus Groups Group discussion on specific topic Rich interaction, efficient, practical Group dynamics bias, moderator influence
Narratives Analysis of personal stories and accounts Understand lived experience, meaning-making Subjective, generalizability limited
Case Study In-depth analysis of single case Detailed understanding, practical insights Limited generalizability, researcher bias
Ethnography Immersion in culture/community Holistic understanding, insider perspective Time-intensive, observer effects, subjectivity

  • Observation
  • Survey
  • Case study
  • Experiment
  • Narratives
  • Focus groups
  • Grounded theory
  • Naturalistic/Field studies

Statistics in Psychology ▼

Descriptive Statistics Organize, summarize, describe data characteristics.
Measures of Central Tendency Describe middle/typical value:
Measure Definition Formula Use
Mean Average of all values Σ X / N Most common; affected by extremes
Median Middle value when ordered - Less affected by outliers
Mode Most frequently occurring value - Categorical data, skewed distributions
Measures of Dispersion/Variability Describe spread of data:
Measure Definition Formula Use
Range Highest - Lowest value Max - Min Quick estimate; crude measure
Variance Average squared deviation from mean Σ(X - M)² / N Foundation for other statistics
Standard Deviation Square root of variance √Variance Most common; same units as data
Normal Probability Curve (Normal Distribution)
  • Bell-shaped, symmetric distribution
  • Mean = Median = Mode
  • 68% data within ±1 SD, 95% within ±2 SD, 99.7% within ±3 SD
  • Used for standardization and interpretation
  • Basis for many statistical tests
Parametric Tests Assume normal distribution, homogeneity of variance, interval/ratio data:
t-test Compares means of two groups:
  • Independent t-test: Two different groups
  • Paired t-test: Same group measured twice
  • Formula: t = (M1 - M2) / SE
  • Assumptions: Normal distribution, equal variances
Non-parametric Tests No assumptions about distribution; use ranks:
  • Sign Test: Compare paired data, simple
  • Wilcoxon Signed Rank Test: Paired data, more powerful
  • Mann-Whitney U Test: Compare two independent groups
  • Kruskal-Wallis Test: Compare multiple independent groups
  • Friedman Test: Compare multiple related groups
Power Analysis and Effect Size
  • Statistical Power: Probability of detecting real effect; typically 0.80 (80%)
  • Type I Error (α): Rejecting true null hypothesis; typically 0.05
  • Type II Error (β): Failing to reject false null hypothesis
  • Effect Size: Magnitude of difference/relationship; Cohen's d, r, eta-squared
  • Sample Size: Larger needed for smaller effect sizes and lower power
Correlational Analysis Examining relationships between variables:
Type Use Range Interpretation
Product Moment (Pearson r) Two continuous variables -1 to +1 r² = proportion of variance shared
Rank Order (Spearman rho) Ordinal data or non-normal -1 to +1 Non-parametric version of r
Partial Correlation Relationship controlling third variable -1 to +1 Effect of confounding variables removed
Multiple Correlation One DV with multiple IVs 0 to 1 Prediction from multiple predictors
Special Correlation Methods
  • Biserial: One continuous, one dichotomous variable (assumed continuous)
  • Point Biserial: One continuous, one truly dichotomous variable
  • Tetrachoric: Two dichotomous variables (assumed continuous)
  • Phi Coefficient: Two truly dichotomous variables
Regression Analysis
Simple Linear Regression
  • Predicting one DV from one IV
  • Equation: Y' = a + bX
  • b = regression coefficient (slope)
  • a = intercept (Y when X = 0)
  • R² = proportion of variance in Y explained by X
Multiple Regression
  • Predicting one DV from multiple IVs
  • Equation: Y' = a + b₁X₁ + b₂X₂ + b₃X₃...
  • Each b shows effect of that predictor
  • R² = total variance explained
  • Adjusted R² = correction for number of predictors
Factor Analysis Identifying underlying factors/dimensions in data:
  • Assumptions: Correlations exist, sample size > 100, normal distribution
  • Exploratory FA: Discover factors
  • Confirmatory FA: Test pre-specified factor structure
  • Rotation Methods: Orthogonal (factors uncorrelated), Oblique (correlated)
  • Factor Loadings: Correlation of variable with factor
  • Interpretation: Group variables by loadings on factors
Experimental Designs
ANOVA (Analysis of Variance) Compares means across multiple groups:
  • One-way ANOVA: One IV with 2+ levels; basic omnibus test
  • Factorial ANOVA: Multiple IVs; examines main effects and interactions
  • Assumptions: Normal distribution, homogeneity of variance, independence
  • F-ratio: Between-group variance / Within-group variance
  • Post-hoc Tests: Tukey, Bonferroni (if significant ANOVA)
Advanced Designs
  • Randomized Block Design: Blocks out confounding variable
  • Repeated Measures Design: Same subjects, multiple measurements
  • Latin Square: Controls two confounding variables
  • Cohort Studies: Following groups over time
  • Time Series: Multiple measurements over extended time
  • MANOVA: Multiple dependent variables
  • ANCOVA: Control for covariate before analyzing main IV
  • Single-Subject Designs: N-of-1; intense study of individual
Key Point Choose statistical test based on: number of IVs/DVs, groups, data level, distribution, and assumptions met.
Statistical Tests Decision Tree
  • 2 groups, parametric → t-test
  • 2 groups, non-parametric → Mann-Whitney
  • 3+ groups, parametric → ANOVA
  • 3+ groups, non-parametric → Kruskal-Wallis
  • Correlation → Pearson r or Spearman rho
  • Prediction → Regression

💡 Key Questions for Revision

Q1: Explain the differences between parametric and non-parametric tests.

Answer:

Parametric Tests:

  • Assume specific distribution (usually normal)
  • Require homogeneity of variance
  • Use interval/ratio data
  • More powerful (better at detecting real effects)
  • Examples: t-test, ANOVA, Pearson correlation

Non-parametric Tests:

  • Make no assumptions about distribution
  • Use rank-ordered data
  • Work with ordinal/categorical data
  • Less powerful than parametric alternatives
  • Examples: Mann-Whitney U, Kruskal-Wallis, Wilcoxon

When to use: Use parametric if assumptions met; non-parametric if data non-normal or small sample

Q2: What is effect size and why is it important in psychology research?

Answer:

Definition: Effect size quantifies magnitude of difference or relationship; independent of sample size.

Common Measures:

  • Cohen's d - standardized difference between means
  • Correlation coefficient r - strength of relationship
  • Eta-squared (η²) - proportion of variance explained

Importance:

  • Statistical significance ≠ practical significance
  • Large samples can show significant but small effects
  • Effect size aids meta-analysis
  • Needed for power analysis and sample size calculation
  • Helps interpret real-world importance of findings
Q3: Distinguish between correlation and causation. Why is this distinction important?

Answer:

Correlation: Two variables covary/relationship exists; r coefficient shows strength and direction.

Causation: Changes in one variable cause changes in other; temporal precedence and exclusion of alternatives required.

Why Distinction Matters:

  • Correlational studies cannot establish causation
  • Third variables may explain relationship (confounds)
  • Direction of causality unclear in correlation
  • Only experimental designs can establish causation
  • Misinterpreting causation leads to wrong conclusions and policies
